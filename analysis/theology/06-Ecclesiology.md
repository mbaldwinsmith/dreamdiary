# Ecclesiology: The Church, Sacraments, and the Community of Faith

## Overview

Ecclesiology — the theology of the Church — asks what the community of believers is, what it does, how it worships, and what authority it carries. For this dreamer, the Church appears across the corpus in a range of registers: as physical building, as sacramental community, as eschatological Body, as flawed institution, and as the cosmic *Third Temple* being assembled in preparation for the final coming of Christ. The dreamer's ecclesiology is generous, high-church in its sacramental instincts, charismatic in its pneumatology, and robustly apostolic in its missiology — a synthesis that does not fit neatly into any single tradition but draws from several.

---

## I. The Church as Building and Body: A Sustained Ambivalence

The Church appears frequently as a *physical building* in the dreams, and the dreamer's relationship to it is rarely straightforward. In *Renewal Dream*:

> *"I dreamt that I was in a city or large town, staying in a Church. The Church generated its own energy, had a lively social presence, and even had its own swimming pool."*

The self-generating energy of this Church — not dependent on external power, operating with its own vitality — is an ecclesiology of the Holy Spirit: the Church as a community whose vitality is constitutive rather than derived from external culture. The swimming pool as the site of *children's lessons and baptisms* places the sacramental life of initiation at the Church's centre. The Church is not primarily a concert hall or a lecture room but a *font* — a place where people are washed into new identity.

Yet the dreamer's failure in this same dream — walking in unclean, muddying the pool, rationalising his uncleanliness — is a reminder that even those with spiritual status (*knights*) can profane the sacred space by insufficient self-examination. The Church requires not merely presence but *prepared presence*.

In *Driving Away Demons*, the dreamer ends up at a *"peaceful, candlelit graveyard"* — a sacred space associated with death and therefore with resurrection — after the spiritual combat. The Church's sacramental life surrounds the realities of death; its peace is not naïve but *post-combat*, hard-won.

## II. The Prayers of the Saints: The Church as Structural Prayer

*Prayers of the Saints* contains one of the most architecturally precise ecclesiological images in the corpus:

> *"Vivid image of a gothic church, supported by two flying buttresses, representing both of our prayers edifying and supporting the body of Christ. A reminder that Christ builds His church upon the prayers of the saints."*

The *flying buttress* is a medieval architectural innovation: an external arch that counteracts the outward thrust of a vaulted ceiling, allowing the walls to be thinner and the windows larger, flooding the interior with light. That the dreamer sees the prayers of two saints as flying buttresses is a theology of *structural intercession*: prayer does not merely address God vertically but supports the *horizontal structure* of the Church, enabling it to bear greater weight of grace and to admit more light. Without the buttresses, the vault collapses; without the prayers of the saints, the Church loses structural integrity.

The dual buttresses also suggest *complementarity*: neither Rosemarie's prayers alone nor the dreamer's alone are sufficient — they work together, on opposite sides of the building, to hold the structure up. Ecclesiology is therefore *relational* at its foundation: the Church is built by the prayers of *people praying for each other*, not by individual spiritual achievement.

Matthew 16:18 — *"on this rock I will build my church"* — is explicitly echoed. The dream affirms that Christ *builds* the Church; the agency is his. But the *material* he uses is the prayers of the saints — human collaboration with divine architecture.

## III. The Sacrament of Communion: Vampire Priest

*Vampire Priest* is the most theologically provocative ecclesiological dream in the corpus:

> *"World of vampires and priests. Inquisitor-like organisation in the church, which hunts down vampires. If vampires don't feed, they become less and less human and crave more. Vampire called Mystra harboured by a church, who give her real blood during communion. Priest who allows Mystra to feed on him. Another priest becomes a vampire, but communion dulls his cravings."*

This is an extended sacramental allegory. The *vampire's need for blood* is analogous to the human spiritual need for genuine nourishment — a need that, if unmet, produces progressive dehumanisation and increasing craving. The Church's response — offering *real blood during communion* — is a theology of the Eucharist as the only adequate response to the soul's hungers. The Real Presence doctrine (that Christ is genuinely present in the consecrated elements, body and blood) is directly at stake: the vampires need *real blood*, not a symbol of blood, and the Church provides it.

The priest who *allows Mystra to feed on him* is a striking image of pastoral *kenosis* (self-emptying): giving one's own lifeblood to nourish the spiritually desperate. The priest who *becomes a vampire* but finds his cravings *dulled by communion* enacts the logic of 1 Corinthians 11:23–26 — the Eucharist as the ongoing appropriation of Christ's sacrifice, quenching the soul's destructive thirst by meeting it with the real thing.

The *Inquisitor-like organisation* that hunts vampires is the Church's institutional self-policing — well-intentioned but potentially dangerous in its rigidity. It does not understand that some vampires can be redeemed through sacramental provision rather than exterminated through ecclesiastical power. This is an ecclesiological critique: the Church's punitive and exclusionary instincts, when dominant over its sacramental and pastoral instincts, miss the very souls it is equipped to save.

## IV. Holy Mass in Heaven: The Eschatological Liturgy

*Grandad Paddy* provides a glimpse of the Church's eschatological form:

> *"I'm in the spiritual realm. Attended Holy Mass in heaven with Pope Benedict and the saints."*

This is the *Liturgy of the Church Triumphant* — the worship of the Church in its glorified, heavenly form. That the dreamer *attends* this Mass suggests his participation in the full scope of the Church's existence, which extends beyond death. Hebrews 12:22–24 describes this: *"you have come to Mount Zion, to the city of the living God, the heavenly Jerusalem... to the assembly of the firstborn, whose names are written in heaven... to the spirits of the righteous made perfect."*

Pope Benedict's presence is ecclesiologically significant: the papacy as an institution is present in the heavenly liturgy, affirming a vision of the Church that is *institutional as well as charismatic*, *historically continuous* as well as *eschatologically transformed*. The dreamer's relationship to Catholicism — deeply informed by Catholic sacramental theology, Marian devotion, and the communion of saints — is here shown its heavenly form.

The sub-dream within *Grandad Paddy* — *"the supermarket of sin"* — located within the context of the heavenly Mass, makes a structural point: the Mass is the *eschatological counter-image* of the Supermarket. Where the Supermarket is chaotic, hungry, and stocked with counterfeits, the Mass is ordered, nourishing, and offers the genuine article — the body and blood of Christ, the only food that satisfies.

## V. The Third Temple: Ecclesiology as Eschatology

The *Herald of the King* dream's ecclesiology is its most radical:

> *"Children of God are literally the body of Christ and, together, form the Third Temple. The second coming will be preceded by a mass awakening of Christ's body and the Holy Spirit's temple. We will act as one body under Christ, forming the frontline of His kingdom conquest."*

The *Third Temple* expectation in Jewish and Christian eschatology has historically been understood as a physical structure to be built in Jerusalem. The dreamer's dream-revelation reframes it: the Third Temple *is the corporate Body of Christ*, filled with the Holy Spirit. This is consistent with Paul's language in 1 Corinthians 3:16 (*"Don't you know that you yourselves are God's temple and that God's Spirit dwells in your midst?"*) and Ephesians 2:19–22 (*"you are... being built together to become a dwelling in which God lives by his Spirit"*).

The eschatological dimension — that the Church-as-Temple is the *precondition for Christ's return* — is the claim that pushes this beyond standard ecclesiology into prophetic territory. The Church is not merely the community of believers or the guardian of sacraments but the *architectural material of the final age*: the building whose completion signals the readiness for the Lord's return. This gives ecclesial participation an urgency and an eschatological weight it rarely carries in ordinary parish life.

## VI. The Megachurch and the Family: Ecclesiology in Tension

*Jesus Is Lord* contains a revealing ecclesiological tension. The dream begins in *"a huge auditorium, bigger than C3, like a MegaChurch"* — a large contemporary evangelical church context — but the action that matters happens *outside* it: in a field, in a confrontation with family, in the dreamer's own interior struggle over whether to keep proclaiming Christ.

Megan's interpretation, included in the dream record, points to this:

> *"So the fact you're in a bigger auditorium could represent the wider world outside of church? Like with your mum and dad — could show the religious application outside of C3 setting and that gap that God has created for you to sow the word of God outside in this other opportunity."*

The ecclesiological insight is that the Church *in its auditorium* is only the beginning of the dreamer's vocation — the real field of action is the *wider world*, especially the unbelieving family. The megachurch gradually fills with participants who are all part of the show (suggesting the broad evangelical community finding its role) while the dreamer and his parents remain apart — the difficult, irreducibly personal mission field that no institutional programme can address.

The *hacking of Dad's phone* — showing all the Christian content he had privately consumed — is an ecclesiological motif too: the Church's influence extends into the private devotional lives of those who would not identify as its members. The Spirit is building his temple without necessarily informing the human beings through whom he is doing it.

## VII. The Silver Crucifix: Ecclesiology as Resistance to Ambition

*Snippets* includes an arresting ecclesiological warning:

> *"Long pillared gallery full of stone slabs with dead senior church leaders in glittering robes lying on them. Many of the rather bloated, gaudy-looking figures wore extravagant gold and bejewelled crucifixes. Every time I reached out to touch a gold crucifix, it turned black and crumbled. Around my own neck was a simple silver crucifix. I felt strongly told not to go for the gold of high ecclesiastic office."*

This is an ecclesiological theology of *vocation* and *ambition*. The gold crucifixes — the symbols of episcopal and high ecclesiastical office — turn black and crumble when touched: they are *false goods*, *pseudo-rubedo* in alchemical terms, the counterfeit completion of the vocation-work. The dreamer's silver crucifix is *simpler and more genuine*: not the absence of authority but the right kind of authority, untarnished by institutional ambition.

The *bloated and gaudy* figures are the ones who reached for the gold: they are dead now, lying on slabs, their excessive vestments oddly pathetic in retrospect. The ecclesiological message is clear: high office is not the goal; fidelity to the genuine calling is the goal. The Church's institutional hierarchy can be as much a spiritual trap as a spiritual vehicle.

## VIII. The Monks at the HUGS Sign: Ecclesiology as Hospitality

In *Snippets*, the *Hugs* section offers a brief but warm ecclesiological image:

> *"Lucid dream in a city market. Followed a fluorescent sign saying HUGS which pointed to a line of friendly-looking cowled monks near a large red-brick building."*

The monks — religious, set apart, ordered — are encountered not through their doctrine or their institutional authority but through the simple offer of *physical embrace*. This is the monastic tradition at its best: the contemplative community as a source of radical hospitality. The fluorescent sign is anachronistic and commercial in one sense (HUGS as a shop sign) but the dreamer follows it to something genuinely human and sacred: religious community as the place where one is *held*.

This stands in gentle counterpoint to the ambition dream: where the gold crucifixes of high office crumble, the simple offer of embracing strangers leads to genuine communal presence.

## IX. Theological Evolution

**Early phase — The Church as building and institution**: The dreamer inhabits churches, attends masses, observes their social energy and spatial grandeur. The Church is primarily *there* — a given structure to be moved through.

**Middle phase — The Church as sacramental community**: The sacramental dimensions deepen: communion as genuine nourishment, the prayers of saints as structural support, the font as the centre of identity. The Church is not just a building but a *nourishing community* whose practices carry genuine spiritual efficacy.

**Mature phase — The Church as eschatological Body**: The Church is revealed as the *Third Temple*, the *Body of Christ*, the *frontline of Kingdom conquest*. Ecclesiology becomes eschatology: the Church's existence is not merely present-tense community but the *architectural material of the age to come*.

---

## X. Synthesis

The ecclesiology of this corpus is characterised by:

- **Sacramental seriousness**: baptism, Eucharist, and intercession are genuinely efficacious, not symbolic; they meet real spiritual hungers
- **Architectural theology**: the Church is a building sustained by the buttresses of prayer, and its inner space is the Temple of the Spirit
- **Institutional ambivalence**: the Church's institutional form can be life-giving (monks offering hugs, priests sustaining vampires through communion) or deadening (the Inquisitor, the bloated bishops with their gold crucifixes)
- **Eschatological urgency**: the Church's ultimate identity is as the Third Temple, the Body awakening toward Christ's return
- **Communal interdependence**: the Church is built through mutual prayer and mutual proclamation, not individual spiritual achievement
- **Vocational humility**: the silver crucifix over the gold; the field over the auditorium; the prayers over the office

This is a Catholic ecclesiology in its instincts (sacrament, saints, liturgical tradition) cross-pollinated with charismatic and evangelical energy (the awakening Body, the apostolic mission, the proclaimed name) — a synthesis that reflects a biography of spiritual formation across multiple traditions.
