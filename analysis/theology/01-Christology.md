# Christology: The Person and Work of Jesus Christ

## Overview

No theological theme runs more insistently through this dream corpus than the figure of Jesus Christ. He appears as cosmic rescuer, name of power, debated proposition, mystical companion, alchemical type, Logos made flesh, and returning King. Taken together, the Christological material in these dreams describes a theology that is simultaneously orthodox, experiential, and strikingly personal — a faith that did not originate in catechism but in encounter, and that continues to be refined through direct visionary experience.

---

## I. Pre-Christian Logos: The Seed Before the Name

The Christological journey begins, unusually, *before* conversion. In *Atheist Logos*, the dreamer recalls:

> *"I believed in the Logos while still an atheist. Trinity of Source, Logos-Sophia, and Presence/Breath. Impersonal principles, not people. Later discovered Jesus as Logos-Sophia through the Gospel of John."*

This is a theologically extraordinary datum. The dreamer arrived at something structurally equivalent to the Johannine prologue through reason alone — an ordering principle (*Logos*), a creative wisdom (*Sophia*), and an immanent presence (*Breath/Pneuma*) — before any Christian confession. The Gospel of John then functioned not as the introduction of a new idea but as the *naming of what was already known*. This is Origen's *logos spermatikos* playing out in a single biography: the universal Logos scattered as seeds throughout human reason, crystallising into the particular Logos of Bethlehem.

The significance for Christology is profound. For this dreamer, Christ is not primarily a moral teacher or a religious tradition but the *cosmic principle of rational order* that becomes personal. The Greek Word and the Galilean carpenter are one. This gives his Christology an unusually philosophical depth from the outset — he comes to Jesus not through guilt or need but through ontological recognition.

---

## II. Christ as the Name of Power

The most dramatically recurring Christological motif is the *name of Jesus as operative force in spiritual warfare*. In *Driving Away Demons by Calling on Jesus*:

> *"Occasionally, when they were gaining on me, I'd turn and call out to Jesus to protect me and they'd be flung back and stunned... I fell to my knees, facing back towards the path and just prayed — through my eyelids, I saw a burst of light and a beautiful chorus of song broke out from all around me."*

This is not merely petitionary prayer; it is the classical Christian doctrine of the *nomen Jesu* as exorcistic power — rooted in Philippians 2:9–11 ("at the name of Jesus every knee shall bow") and the apostolic practice in Acts. The demons do not flee from the dreamer's virtue, courage, or knowledge; they flee from *the name*. The dreamer himself notes his subsequent regret: *"I regretted not telling them to explicitly call on the name of Jesus"* — a theologically precise recognition that the power is located in the name specifically, not in vague theistic invocation.

The same dynamic appears in *Temptation in the Garden*, where demonic women — beautiful, seductive, ultimately menacing — circle the dreamer and cannot touch him while he repeats *the Jesus Prayer*. The Prayer ("Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner") is the classical formula of Eastern Orthodox mystical practice, understood as a *mantra of the Name*. That the dreamer uses it in both lucid spiritual combat and in the *Heavenly Ascension* dream (reciting it underwater) suggests a developing Hesychast sensibility: the Name as the centre of gravity of the interior life.

In *Series of Doors*, the dreamer is lost in a labyrinth of doors and repeats *"Kyrie Eleison"* — "Lord, have mercy" — until *"an image of Christ"* appears and the last door opens onto a church. The Name opens what cannot otherwise be opened. This is Christology as *key* — not merely as idea but as operative reality.

## III. The Embodied Encounter: Ascension and the Gothic Citadel

The most visually spectacular Christological material appears in *Heavenly Ascension*:

> *"I was carried up into the heavens, surrounded by a golden circle of symbols and faces, beautiful song and an image of Jesus, carrying me in His arms. I tingled all over, and felt an elated feeling, like living water, course through my body."*

> *"I went into a gothic citadel with Jesus, guarded by some armoured figures. Some of the armoured figures would go for me, recognising me as an unclean outsider, but Jesus would command them to stop."*

Two distinct aspects of Christ emerge here. First, Christ as the *one who carries*: the dreamer is passive, borne upward, cradled. This is the Christology of Isaiah 63:9 (*"he lifted them up and carried them"*) and Psalm 91 — the maternal, sheltering aspect of the saviour that does not require the believer to ascend under his own power. The tingling sensation and the *"living water"* language evokes John 4 and 7, where Christ promises water welling up to eternal life — here it is experienced as physical sensation, a somatic Christophany.

Second, Christ as the *authority within the holy place*: in the citadel, the armoured guardians (presumably angelic) recognise the dreamer as *unclean* and move to expel him. Christ intervenes — not by declaring the dreamer clean, but by *commanding* them to stop. The distinction is theologically precise: the dreamer is not worthy of the holy place on his own merits; he is *admitted by Christ's authority*. This is imputed righteousness, forensic justification — not his sanctity but Christ's command admits him. The gothic citadel, gilded and glittering, is described as *beautiful*: holiness is aesthetically overwhelming, not abstractly moral.

In *Jesus Is Lord*, the dreamer is swept into the sky again:

> *"I was lifted up into the sky, surrounded by golden light and a beautiful scene with a chorus of angels all singing in harmony, 'Jesus is Lord!'"*

And again the Name is the hinge. The vision comes *immediately after* the dreamer proclaims it — personal confession triggers theophanic response. Christ's Lordship is not a proposition to be debated but a reality that manifests when spoken aloud.

## IV. Christ as Logos of Reality: The Dream Prison and the Fractal Self

In *Dream Prison*, after years of being locked in a virtual prison, the dreamer reaches a decisive moment:

> *"I aligned my fractal image to that of Christ and became Lord Protector of part of the Kingdom of God covering the UK."*

This is the most theologically dense Christological statement in the corpus. The *fractal image* evokes Colossians 1:15 and Genesis 1:26–27: Christ as the *image (eikon) of the invisible God*, humanity made in the image (*imago Dei*) of God. To *align one's fractal image to that of Christ* is to undergo conformatio Christi — the classical spiritual theology of being conformed to the likeness of the Son (Romans 8:29). The mathematical language (*fractal*) is not a secularisation but a deepening: the self-similar structure of the divine image replicated at every scale of human existence.

The political result — *Lord Protector*, reforming democracy, serving as *Vicar* (Viceroy to Christ) — places this firmly within a *Christus Rex* theology: Christ's sovereignty over nations and structures, mediated through human vicegerents. The dreamer acts not as a theocrat imposing religion but as a representative of Christ's legitimate sovereignty: *"restoring his sovereignty while also honouring popular sovereignty."* This is a sophisticated political theology that distinguishes divine authority from human tyranny.

## V. The Christ Cycle: Types, Echoes, and the Unique Particular

The *Christ Cycle* dream contains what may be the most theologically articulate single passage in the entire corpus:

> *"Types of Christ and prophets continually born, refining the type, pruning off false Messiahs, false prophets and false prophecies. Truth is distilled by the cycle and the perfect character of God demonstrated... Jesus literally unique, one of a kind, despite types... types are not literal Christs or literal crucifixions. One Christ, one true crucifixion, many echoes, many types, deeply intimate, sacred experiences. All comes together perfectly only once."*

This is a fully developed *typological* Christology in the tradition of Justin Martyr and Irenaeus. History produces *prefigurations* — partial, preparatory, genuine intimations of the archetype — but these point toward and are fulfilled in a single unrepeatable event. The logic is both historical (the types come before; Christ comes after and completes) and ontological (the Incarnation is not one instance of a repeatable pattern but the singular enfleshment of the eternal Logos).

The phrase *"all comes together perfectly only once"* has the precision of a theological axiom. It simultaneously affirms the validity of typological reading (the types are real, the echoes are sacred) and guards against any inflation of those types into equivalence with the original. The dreamer's insight — arrived at in dream, not in seminar — recapitulates a patristic consensus it may never have consciously studied.

## VI. The Resurrection as Non-Negotiable: Arguing About Resurrection

In *Arguing About Resurrection*, the dreamer finds himself in a theological debate:

> *"I said that such beliefs explicitly contradict our founding credal statements and that it's really very simple: either Jesus is who He says He is or He isn't."*

> *"In all of this tip-toeing around, trying to avoid causing offense, we must watch out that we're not offending the only One Whose opinion actually matters."*

The statue of Jesus present in the dream — *"with a dark mantle of stars and space, his human body gleaming with soft ethereal light"* — is itself a Christological image: the cosmic Christ who wears the universe as a garment, yet whose *human body* remains distinct and luminous. He is not dissolved into the cosmos; he inhabits it as Lord.

The dreamer's theological instinct here is classically *homoousian*: the Resurrection is not an optional metaphor but the constitutive fact of Christian identity. To abandon it is not to offer a more generous Christianity but to change the subject entirely. The "tolerance" argument — that one should not insist on the Resurrection — is gently but firmly refused: the dreamer's concern is not winning an argument but *the salvation of living souls*, a pastoral Christology that grounds its urgency in love.

## VII. The Second Coming: A Collective Christology

The *Herald of the King* dream reframes eschatological Christology in a striking way:

> *"Children of God are literally the body of Christ and, together, form the Third Temple. The second coming will be preceded by a mass awakening of Christ's body and the Holy Spirit's temple. We will act as one body under Christ, forming the frontline of His kingdom conquest. This means that the first stages of the Second Coming are already underway!"*

This is a *corporate* Christology: the Body of Christ (the Church) is not merely a metaphor for communal belonging but the *literal, ongoing presence of Christ in the world*. The Second Coming is not simply an external event to be awaited passively but a process already initiated in the awakening of the Body. This resonates with Eastern Orthodox theologies of *theosis* (deification) and with Paul's language in Ephesians 4 — the Church growing up into Christ, its head.

The *Chosen Ones with the Mark* dream reinforces this with a visionary correction of the Messiah expectation:

> *"There are many Chosen Ones with the mark, not just one Chosen Mark... Christ's return being different than expected: a collective awakening and in-filling of His body on Earth before His full return."*

The dreamer resists the reduction of messianic expectation to a single individual and instead proposes a *distributed Christology* for the last days: many marked ones, a collective anointing, Christ's return as communal participation rather than singular theophany. This is theologically unusual and experientially consistent — it arises not from abstract theology but from dream-vision.

## VIII. The River of Grief: Christ as the Rock of Ages

In *River of Grief*, the dreamer inhabits a fictional Christ-type:

> *"Death, the ultimate warlord struck me down in a final battle. According to the Law, Death must pay a blood price or else lose everything to me. Turns out, I'm the Rock of Ages and my bloodguilt price cannot be paid. I, the rock, split in two and, from me, flows a rushing torrent of all the tears of the slain who now belong to me. Death is forever indebted and all death owns, including the souls of the dead, belong to me."*

This is one of the most theologically striking passages in the corpus. The logic is forensic and mythic simultaneously: Death has legal rights over those it has slain; but the Rock of Ages has a *blood debt* that cannot be repaid; therefore all Death's holdings must be surrendered. The imagery conflates multiple streams — the cosmic Christ of Colossians 1 who holds all things together, the *lex talionis* of the Law, the Christus Victor of Aulen's atonement theology, and the Rock of Moses (1 Corinthians 10:4) from which living water flows.

The *tears of the slain* flowing from the split rock is among the most emotionally saturated images in the corpus: resurrection is not merely juridical vindication but the *gathering and vindication of every grief*. This Christ does not merely legally defeat death; he *owns* all the dead as their liberator.

## IX. Theological Evolution

The Christological arc of this corpus can be mapped across three stages:

**Stage 1 — Logos without a face**: An intellectual recognition of an ordering principle (Logos-Sophia) that operates impersonally, arrived at through philosophical reasoning before conversion.

**Stage 2 — The named and operative Christ**: Conversion through John's Gospel gives the Logos a name and a face. The name becomes experientially operative — repelling demonic attack, opening locked doors, lifting the dreamer skyward. Christology becomes *practical* and *liturgical* rather than purely philosophical.

**Stage 3 — Corporate and eschatological Christ**: The dreamer's Christology widens from personal encounter to cosmic scope. Christ is the archetype whom all types refine. His body is a collective organism awakening toward his return. His victory over Death is universal in scope, gathering all the slain. The dreamer participates in this Christ not merely as a devotee but as a *type*, a *herald*, a *viceroy*, a *member of his body*.

The movement is from the impersonal to the personal to the corporate — from Logos as principle, to Jesus as saviour and companion, to Christ as the unifying head of a people and a cosmos. This is not abandonment of earlier stages but their successive deepening.

---

## X. Synthesis

The Christology of these dreams is simultaneously:

- **Johannine**: rooted in Logos theology, the Gospel of John as conversion catalyst, Christ as the Word that structures reality
- **Orthodox**: the Jesus Prayer as central practice, Christ carrying the dreamer in arms, angelic reverence
- **Charismatic**: the Name as operative power, spiritual warfare, visible and physical experiences of Christ's presence
- **Typological**: the Christ Cycle as ongoing distillation through history
- **Corporate and eschatological**: the Body forming the Third Temple, the Second Coming already in motion
- **Atonement-centred**: Christ as the Rock whose blood price cannot be repaid, Death's legal defeat

No single Christological tradition exhausts what these dreams present. They suggest a theology assembled not from study alone but from encounter — the dreamer meeting Christ in the night, naming what he finds, and discovering that the ancient tradition has words for all of it.

## The Christology of the Poems

The poems supply what the dreams, with their mythological freight and dramatic imagery, cannot always render: the *voice of Christ himself*, speaking directly about his own identity and work. Three poems in particular constitute a Christological statement of remarkable theological density.

*Since the Beginning* opens with the divine self-declaration: *"I am Alpha and Omega, beginning and end. / Morning Star and the dark shade of evening."* This is Revelation 1:8 and 22:13 given lyric form — Christ as the one who encompasses the full range of temporal experience, from dawn to dusk, from origin to conclusion. *"I hide in plain sight behind every door. / Knock for Me and I shall always answer"* — this is the Logos-Christ of John 14 (*"I am the door"*; *"knock and it will be opened to you"*) rendered lyric: the cosmic Christ simultaneously everywhere and instantly accessible. *"I created the stars and the Sun, / so you'd find me in every one"* — the self-disclosure is deliberate, the scattering through creation an act of *purposive hiding* undertaken for the creature's benefit. This is Christology as the story of the Logos planting himself in everything he made so that his searching creatures could not miss him.

*All of My Creatures* gives the atonement its most concentrated poetic statement: *"All of my creatures have my breath, / for my children I conquered death."* The *breath* of God (the *neshamah* of Genesis 2:7, the *pneuma* of John 20:22) as the animating principle of all creaturely life; the *conquest of death* as the Christological act that liberates what the breath animated. The voice is the Father's yet the act is the Son's — the economic Trinitarian distinction held in a single couplet. This is the *Christus Victor* of *River of Grief* — *"Death is forever indebted and all death owns... belong to me"* — given its lyric compression: conquered, not debated.

*Communion* supplies the Eucharistic Christology that the *Vampire Priest* and *Grandad Paddy* dreams enact in mythological form: *"Meet me at the table, my Lord, / let me feast on You, bread and wine."* The feast is not abstract — it is bread and wine, the material elements of Real Presence. And *"here You raise me from the dead"* — the Eucharistic encounter is itself a resurrection experience, the eating of Christ's body producing the same effect as his Resurrection: the death of death, the life of the receiver. The dreamer who is lifted into the golden citadel and admitted by Christ's command is, in miniature, the soul raised at the table by Christ's Real Presence.

*My Peace I Give to You* develops the political Christology of *Dream Prison* and *Herald of the King* in lyric mode: *"Come to My Father boldly, the curtain is torn."* The torn curtain is the singular eschatological-Christological event of Matthew 27:51 — the opening of the Holy of Holies, the dissolution of the sacred barrier between divine and human. What the dreams enact in gothic citadels with armoured guardians overruled by Christ's command, the poem declares directly: the curtain is already torn. Access is already granted. The Christology of the name — *"pray this in His beautiful and boundless name"* — is here its fullest form: not the name as emergency weapon but the name as the medium of the tear, the point of entry into the Father's presence.

*For Love, In Love, He Made You* adds a temporal dimension the other Christological poems lack: *"Since before the beginning of time, / He's had you in His mind."* This is eternal election (Ephesians 1:4) — Christ as the one in whom humanity was chosen *before the foundation of the world*. *"The Word plotted out your soul's climb"* — the Logos-Sophia who appears in *Atheist Logos* as the ordering principle of reality was, the poem insists, always already personal, always attending to each soul's specific trajectory. The dreamer's pre-conversion encounter with the impersonal Logos was genuine meeting with a person who had always already known him. The *Christ Cycle*'s typological distillation of revelation through history is here given its personal application: the cosmic process was, all along, specifically about *this* soul.
