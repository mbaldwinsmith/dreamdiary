# Mystical Theology: Prayer, Direct Encounter, and the Contemplative Life

## Overview

Mystical theology — the study of direct, experiential encounter with the divine — is not a peripheral concern in this corpus but its animating centre. The dreams are not merely about theological *propositions* but about *experiences*: the sensation of being carried into the heavens, the tingling of living water moving through the body, the Jesus Prayer recited underwater, the dew tasted in a glade, the kiss of the holy graveyard at the forest's edge. The dreamer is, before he is a theologian or a herald or a knight, a *contemplative* — one for whom the knowledge of God is primarily a matter of direct encounter rather than doctrinal abstraction. This module traces the mystical theology implicit in the corpus: its practices, its experiences, its disciplines, and its development from early encounter through to mature integration.

---

## I. The Aquatic Mysticism: Breath, Prayer, and Immersion

The most recurring image of the mystical life in this corpus is *water*. The dreamer swims, breathes underwater, tastes morning dew, wades through floods of tears, and is carried on living water. The aquatic dimension of the spiritual life operates consistently across multiple dreams.

In *Heavenly Ascension*:

> *"I went for a swim in a stream and could breathe under water. While under water, I started saying the Jesus Prayer!"*

Breathing underwater is physically impossible — which makes it the perfect image of *mystical inversion*: the ordinary rules of survival are suspended in the spiritual realm, and what drowns in nature nourishes in grace. That the dreamer's natural response to this freedom is to *say the Jesus Prayer* shows the prayer's instinctive centrality: it is not a technique deployed when threatened (as in *Temptation in the Garden*) but a *spontaneous expression of immersion* in the divine life. When the conditions of ordinary life are removed, the first movement is toward the Name.

The *Jesus Prayer* — *"Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner"* — is the central practice of Hesychast spirituality, the Eastern Orthodox tradition of interior prayer that seeks the *stillness* (*hesychia*) in which the divine light can be perceived. In the Hesychast tradition, the goal of the prayer is the *theoria* — the vision of the divine uncreated light. That the dreamer's underwater prayer immediately precedes the *Ascension* sequence (being carried into the heavens by Christ) suggests a causal relationship: the interior prayer opens the contemplative perception that enables the vision.

In *Lucid City and Beach*:

> *"Said the Jesus prayer a few times. Saw someone who looked like Jesus. Met a fellow Christian, who brought me down to the beach. I stripped off and went swimming."*

The sequence is again aquatic: prayer leads to christophany (seeing someone who looks like Jesus), human communion (the fellow Christian), and finally total immersion (swimming). The *stripping off* before swimming is baptismal: a return to the state of unclothed, undefended human being, the state of Eden and of the newly baptised.

## II. The Morning Dew: Erotic Mysticism and the Danger of Substitution

*Morning Dew* contains the most erotically charged mystical passage in the corpus:

> *"While we made love, I leaned forward and licked the morning dew off the plants on the bank, as the cool water now ran over our enmeshed bodies. The dew was the sweetest, most tangy and refreshing taste I've ever experienced, second only to pleasuring past lovers with my mouth."*

The tradition of erotic mysticism — from the Song of Solomon through Bernard of Clairvaux's *Sermons on the Song of Songs* to John of the Cross's *Spiritual Canticle* — has consistently used the language of erotic love as the closest human analogy to the soul's union with God. The morning dew has its own mystical resonance: Psalm 110:3 describes the Messiah born *"from the womb of the dawn, like the dew"*; Hosea 14:5 promises that God will be *"like the dew to Israel"*; Proverbs 19:12 compares the king's favour to dew on grass.

The dreamer notes the displacement: *"Since then, I've tried looking for non-physical ways to get the same refreshment."* The erotic-mystical experience points beyond itself. The dew is real and genuine — the body is the medium of the mystical encounter — but its sweetness is not exhausted in the physical. The contemplative question the dream raises is: what is the *non-physical equivalent* of this refreshment? This is the classical mystical question of detachment and sublimation: not the denial of the body's experience but the *deepening of it* toward its source in the divine life.

The *red-headed woman* who recurs across the corpus has a similar quality: *"Either my imagination, in my frustration, is devising for my pleasure my perfect woman or a spiritual being of some kind is shaping herself specifically to appeal to me."* The dreamer's uncertainty is theologically honest. The *anima* as mediator of the divine feminine (see the archetypes analysis) and the *succuba* as demonic seductress are not always easily distinguished. The criterion — *"I do not sense anything at all malicious from her"* — is the classical spiritual discernment criterion of *peace*: 1 John 4:1 (*"test the spirits"*) applied phenomenologically.

## III. The Graveyard of Light: Contemplation After Combat

The climax of *Driving Away Demons* is a mystical experience that arrives *after* spiritual combat:

> *"I fell to my knees, facing back towards the path and just prayed. Through my eyelids, I saw a burst of light and a beautiful chorus of song broke out from all around me, when I opened my eyes, the wolf was fleeing into the distance and I was surrounded by indistinct figures of singing light."*

The posture — *falling to the knees*, facing back toward the darkness — is the posture of the contemplative in extremis: total vulnerability, complete dependence on the divine. The eyes are *closed*: the vision comes not through physical sight but through the eyelids, the interior perception that receives light from within rather than from without. This is the classic distinction between *aisthēsis* (sensory perception) and *nous* (intellectual or spiritual perception) — the divine light penetrates the physical darkness.

The *chorus of song* and the *figures of singing light* are the angelic order of worship: the divine presence is always *aesthetic*, always *musical*. The mystic's encounter with God is not cold and conceptual but *warm, luminous, and resonant*. The graveyard — the place of the dead — becomes the site of the most intense experience of life and beauty. This is the paschal theology of mystical experience: the deepest encounter with the divine comes in the place of death, because resurrection is the theology of the graveyard.

## IV. The Heavenly Ascension: The Mystical Union

The Ascension sequence in *Heavenly Ascension* is the most explicitly theophanic experience in the corpus:

> *"I was carried up into the heavens, surrounded by a golden circle of symbols and faces, beautiful song and an image of Jesus, carrying me in His arms. I tingled all over, and felt an elated feeling, like living water, course through my body."*

The language of *tingling*, *elation*, and *living water* coursing through the body is the phenomenology of contemplative experience in the Christian tradition. Teresa of Ávila describes similar sensations in *The Interior Castle*: physical warmth, tingling, a sense of being suspended. John of the Cross speaks of the *"living flame of love"* that touches the soul's centre. The *living water* language is Johannine (John 4:10–14; 7:38–39) and is consistently associated with the Holy Spirit — the experience is Trinitarian even in its somatic register.

That the dreamer is *passive* and *carried* is the mystical theology of *infused contemplation* — the prayer that is given rather than achieved, the grace that overwhelms rather than responds to effort. The distinction in Catholic mystical theology between *acquired* and *infused* contemplation is precisely this: acquired contemplation is the product of disciplined practice; infused contemplation is *given*, descending upon the soul independently of its preparation. The dreamer is not climbing into the heavens but being *lifted*.

The golden circle of *symbols and faces* suggests an encounter with the fullness of the divine life — the symbols of the divine attributes, the faces of the angelic order — all surrounding the central image of Jesus. The mystic's ascent is not to an abstract divine principle but to a *person* who *holds* them.

## V. The City of God as Contemplative Vision

In *Prayers of the Saints*, the lucid section offers a brief taste of *theoria* (contemplative vision):

> *"Lucid. Walked around a breathtaking city, with gardens, trees, streams, waterfalls, and beautiful buildings."*

The *breathtaking* quality is precise: the contemplative vision is not merely aesthetically pleasing but *overwhelming*, exceeding the capacity of ordinary perception. The dreamer's *lucidity* in this vision — his full wakefulness, his awareness that he is in an unusual state — is the contemplative clarity that the mystics call *clear seeing*: no projections, no filters, no interpretive interference, just the direct apprehension of what is there.

The city's fusion of natural and architectural beauty echoes the classical mystical theology of creation: the world as *theophany*, every creature as a *word* of God, the whole cosmos as the divine self-disclosure in material form. To walk through the heavenly city is to walk through God's self-expression at its most concentrated.

## VI. The Sensory Richness of Dream Mysticism

Across the corpus, one of the most consistent features of peak spiritual experience is its *sensory completeness*: the dreamer tastes, smells, feels, hears, and sees with unusual vividness.

In *Breaking Out* (Boar sequence):

> *"I took me to a pumpkin spice shop, which I could smell and taste."*

In *Morning Dew*:

> *"The dew was the sweetest, most tangy and refreshing taste I've ever experienced."*

In *Driving Away Demons*:

> *"A beautiful chorus of song broke out from all around me."*

In *Heavenly Ascension*:

> *"I tingled all over, and felt an elated feeling, like living water, course through my body."*

This sensory richness is theologically significant. The mystical tradition has sometimes been hostile to the senses — Platonism tends to treat sensory experience as a distraction from pure intellectual contemplation. But the Christian mystical tradition, rooted in the Incarnation, has consistently affirmed the senses as *capable of spiritual perception*. The body is not an obstacle to encounter with God but a vehicle. The dreamer's most intense encounters with the divine are *somatic* — felt in the tingling, tasted in the dew, heard in the chorus, smelled in the spice.

This is an *incarnational mysticism*: God meets the soul not by bypassing the body but by intensifying its perceptual capacity.

## VII. Lucidity as Spiritual Practice

The dreamer's *lucid dreaming* practice is developed through the corpus as a spiritual discipline in its own right. In *Breaking Out* and *Cycles of Dream Prisons*, the dreamer repeatedly works to maintain and recover lucidity — the meta-awareness within the dream that enables genuine agency:

> *"Moments of Lucidity: Memory bleed. Looking at clocks. Looking at phone. Feedback loop: dream characters' response to Lucidity or being told they're imagined NPCs."*

In the contemplative tradition, the spiritual life is frequently described in terms of *wakefulness*: the spiritual seeker is the one who is *awake* while the world sleeps. Jesus's Gethsemane command — *"Watch and pray"* — is the command to stay lucid when every natural tendency is toward the comfortable unconsciousness of sleep.

The dreamer's techniques for maintaining lucidity (touching surfaces, looking at clocks, repeating the Jesus Prayer) map onto classical contemplative techniques for maintaining the *watchful attention* necessary for genuine prayer:

- **Touching surfaces** = *grounding in the present moment*, the contemplative emphasis on concrete particularity over abstract spirituality
- **Looking at clocks** = *testing the spirit*, the discernment discipline of checking experience against known reality
- **Repeating the Jesus Prayer** = the *prayer of the heart*, the Hesychast method of anchoring consciousness in the Name

The *feedback loop* — dream characters turning hostile when told they are NPCs — is a contemplative warning: the interior landscape of the psyche resists the observer who comes with clarity. The shadows *turn on* the dreamer when he sees them clearly. But clarity remains the goal; the hostility confirms that something important is at stake.

## VIII. The Contemplative Posture: Sitting With the Shadow

Perhaps the most mature mystical moment in the corpus is the *Shadow Integration* sequence:

> *"Suddenly filled with compassion, I try to say I love you but it's muffled. I gently approach the shadow and it recoils. I sit down next to it and wrap my arms around myself in a hug. I just sit, gently present with the shadow."*

This is *apophatic* mystical theology applied to the interior life: the dreamer does not *achieve* anything, does not *say* anything clearly, does not *fight* or *fix*. He simply *sits*. This is the contemplative practice at its deepest level — the prayer that Thomas Merton calls *"a kind of loving awareness"*, the *hesychia* of the Eastern tradition, the *recollection* of the Carmelites. The presence *is* the prayer; the sitting *is* the intervention.

That this practice dissolves the shadow — *"The shadow turns into a watery blob and slowly dissipates"* — is the mystical theology of *love as the deepest ontological force*: stronger than confrontation, stronger than argument, stronger than battle. The shadow does not flee because it is defeated but because it is *met with a love it cannot resist*.

## IX. The Integration of Mystical Experience: Prayer as the Grammar of the Dreaming Life

The *Jesus Is Lord* prayer that ends that dream is the corpus's clearest example of the *integration* of mystical experience into ordinary life through prayer:

> *"Beloved Father, thank you for this dream and for a glimpse of your throne. Thank you for the evidence that my Dad has indeed been feeling his way to faith... Sorry for failing my family and you in stopping proclaiming that you're Lord for fear of angering them... Please, in Your mercy, do not abandon me to them, but equally do not abandon them without me... Heaven and Earth are Yours, and Jesus is truly Lord of all. I pray this in His beautiful and boundless name! Amen."*

This prayer is the contemplative conclusion of the dream: the vision has been received; now it must be *integrated* through prayer. The prayer moves through thanksgiving (for the vision), penitence (for the failures within the vision), intercession (for the family), and doxology (Jesus is Lord of all). It is the classical shape of Christian prayer — ACTS: Adoration, Confession, Thanksgiving, Supplication — applied to the specific content of a single night's vision.

That the dreamer moves immediately from dream to prayer shows the *contemplative reflex* at work: the mystical experience is not complete until it has been *gathered into prayer*, until the soul has responded consciously to what it received unconsciously. This is the integration that transforms vision from entertainment into formation.

## X. Theological Evolution

**Early phase — Reactive mysticism**: The dreamer experiences what the mystics call *extraordinary graces* (luminous figures, angelic chorus, spiritual combat) without yet having a developed contemplative practice. The experiences happen *to* him; he receives and records them.

**Middle phase — Discipline emerging**: The Jesus Prayer becomes a habitual resource, used instinctively in combat (*Temptation*), spontaneously underwater (*Ascension*), and deliberately to anchor lucidity (*Lucid City*). The prayer moves from technique to *temperament*.

**Mature phase — Integrated contemplation**: The sitting with the shadow, the post-vision prayer, the teaching by angels in *Divine Plans* — contemplation is now operative at every level, from the most intimate (sitting with the inner shadow) to the most cosmic (being taught how prayer reshapes reality). The mystical life is no longer episodic (extraordinary encounters) but *pervasive* (the whole of experience is available as material for encounter with God).

---

## XI. Synthesis

The mystical theology of this corpus is characterised by:

- **Aquatic imagery**: immersion, breath, dew, living water — the spiritual life is overwhelmingly liquid, always dissolving the fixed and refreshing the parched
- **Somatic experience**: tingling, tasting, singing, touching — the body is the mystical organ, not its obstacle
- **The Jesus Prayer as central practice**: simultaneously liturgical, contemplative, exorcistic, and protective — the Name as the soul's breathing
- **Passive and active poles**: the dreamer is sometimes *carried* (infused contemplation) and sometimes *working to stay lucid* (acquired contemplation); both are necessary
- **Beauty as the divine mode**: every theophanic encounter is aesthetic — light, song, city, dew, golden circles — God is always beautiful before he is anything else
- **Love as the deepest mystical act**: sitting with the shadow, holding Nana's hand, tasting the dew — union with the divine is mediated through love's particular concrete acts
- **Integration through prayer**: mystical experience is completed in the prayer that gathers and responds to it

This is the corpus of a genuine contemplative — not one who has read the mystics and applied their techniques, but one who has stumbled into the mystical life through dreams and only gradually discovered that the tradition has names for what he has been living.
