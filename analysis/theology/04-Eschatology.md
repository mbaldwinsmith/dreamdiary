# Eschatology: End Times, Second Coming, and the Shape of the Last Things

## Overview

Eschatology — the theology of last things: death, judgment, resurrection, the end of the world, and the new creation — is one of the most extensively developed theological territories in this corpus. The dreamer does not merely receive standard Christian eschatology passively; he *interrogates, refines, and in several instances revises* it through dream-vision. The result is a body of eschatological theology that is orthodox in its core commitments (resurrection, Christ's final sovereignty, the defeat of death, the renewal of creation) but innovative in its *timing*, *structure*, and *political form*. Several dreams contain what appear to be genuine prophetic or visionary data points about the shape of the end.

---

## I. The Apocalyptic Type: Templar Knight in the Ruins

The *Apocalypse* dream opens with the dreamer as a *futuristic templar knight in power armour* — a figure that fuses medieval Christian militarism with science-fictional eschatology:

> *"Post-apocalyptic Fallout-style future. Clearing ruins. Fighting mutants and robots. Salvaging objects from ruins."*

This is the *knight errant of the desolate age* — a classical eschatological figure who persists in faithful action after the collapse of civilisation. The Templar specifically evokes the crusading orders whose identity was bound up with holding sacred ground against encroachment. The post-apocalyptic setting is a visual theology: the world after the *eschaton* (the end-event) is not immediately restored paradise but a period of clearing, fighting, and salvaging — the *messy in-between* of eschatological transition.

The sequel — *"The Forsaken"* — deepens this:

> *"Sucked into the distant future. Tens of thousands of years. No more Christianity. Primitive, living in harmony with nature. Believe they were left behind after the Apocalypse, hence the Forsaken. I have to rewrite the Bible from memory and write Liturgy for use in church with the converted. Spread the good news that salvation is still at hand."*

This is eschatological theology of striking depth. Tens of thousands of years after the Apocalypse, a community exists who believe themselves *forsaken* — abandoned at the moment of cosmic culmination. The dreamer's vocation is not to inform them that they have been *accurately* assessed but to bring the *corrective gospel*: salvation is *still at hand*. This is the eternal availability of grace, even in the remotest post-eschatological future. The rewriting of the Bible from memory is an act of *oral tradition's triumph over textual loss* — what cannot be destroyed is what has been *inhabited* rather than merely recorded.

## II. The Second Coming as Collective Awakening: Herald of the King

The *Herald of the King* dream contains perhaps the single most developed piece of eschatological theology in the corpus:

> *"Children of God are literally the body of Christ and, together, form the Third Temple. The second coming will be preceded by a mass awakening of Christ's body and the Holy Spirit's temple. We will act as one body under Christ, forming the frontline of His kingdom conquest. This means that the first stages of the Second Coming are already underway!"*

This is a *proleptic eschatology* — an understanding that eschatological events are not simply future but are *already beginning*. The standard evangelical schema separates the present age (church growth) from the future age (Christ's return) sharply. The dreamer collapses this: the Body awakening *is* the Second Coming in its preliminary phase. The Third Temple is not a physical building in Jerusalem but the corporate body of Christ's people filled with the Spirit — a *spiritual temple* whose construction is the precondition for the final return.

The *frontline of His kingdom conquest* language is militantly eschatological: the church is not merely waiting for Christ to arrive and do everything but is *participating in the advance* of his reign. This resonates with the postmillennial tradition (the church, empowered by the Spirit, brings the world increasingly under Christ's reign before his physical return) while also containing strongly charismatic elements (the *awakening*, the *infilling*).

The phrase *"different than expected"* recurs across the eschatological material — in *Herald of the King*, in *Chosen Ones with the Mark*, in the general tenor of the Apocalypse dreams. The dreamer's eschatological vocation is partly *corrective*: to announce that the shape of the end differs from standard expectation.

## III. The Many Marked Ones: Distributed Messianism

*Chosen Ones with the Mark* is one of the most eschatologically complex dreams in the corpus:

> *"The Chosen One would be called Mark... there are many Chosen Ones with the mark, not just one Chosen Mark... my post-conversion insight into Christ's return being different than expected: a collective awakening and in-filling of His body on Earth before His full return."*

The dream takes the *Chosen One* prophecy (a stock eschatological figure in both religious and popular culture — echoed explicitly in *Harry Potter*) and *pluralises* it. This is significant: the dreamer resists both the inflation of his own particularity (he refuses to accept the *one* Chosen One designation) and the deflation of his calling (he does not deny being a Chosen One, only that the category is singular). The category is valid but must be democratised.

Theologically, this maps onto a distinction within Jewish and Christian eschatology between *individual* and *corporate* messianism. The Hebrew Bible contains both: the individual Servant of Isaiah 53 and the corporate servant-Israel (Isaiah 41:8). Early Christianity understood Jesus as fulfilling both: he is the individual Messiah *and* his body (the church) is incorporated into his messianic identity. The dreamer's vision extends this: the final phase of Christ's return is not a solitary divine descent but a *collective awakening* in which the many marked ones, each in their measure, participate in Christ's messianic identity.

The *choking* that prevents him from speaking — *"an invisible force choked me. I pushed and bellowed, YOU HAVE THE WRONG MARK"* — is a demonological-eschatological detail: the suppression of corrective eschatological testimony by spiritual resistance. That he overcomes it and bellows the correction suggests that eschatological truth-telling requires effort against spiritual opposition.

## IV. The Kingdom of God: Lord Protector and the New Society

*Dream Prison* presents the most politically developed eschatological vision in the corpus:

> *"I aligned my fractal image to that of Christ and became Lord Protector of part of the Kingdom of God covering the UK. As an enlightened dictator, I reformed democracy, gave more power to the people, and renewed society and the land. I foiled an assassination attempt on the steps of Westminster Abbey, getting the assassin to follow Christ and be baptised there and then. I reigned as a Vicar in the true sense of the word, like a Viceroy to Christ, restoring his sovereignty while also honouring popular sovereignty."*

This is Kingdom of God theology at its most concrete. The *Lord Protector* title deliberately echoes Cromwell — a figure who seized political power claiming divine mandate — but the dream's version is notably different: the dreamer is *explicitly subordinate to Christ* (a *Viceroy*, not a sovereign), and he governs by *giving more power to the people* rather than consolidating it. The tension between divine sovereignty (*restoring his sovereignty*) and popular sovereignty (*honouring popular sovereignty*) is precisely the tension Christian political theology has wrestled with from Augustine through Calvin to the present.

The Westminster Abbey baptism — an assassination attempt converted into a moment of evangelism and sacrament — is eschatological: the last-enemy logic of death and violence is converted, in situ, into life. The *renewal of society and the land* resonates with Romans 8:19–23 (the creation groaning for the sons of God to be revealed) and the *Kingdom* parables of Matthew: the mustard seed that becomes a tree, the leaven that transforms the whole lump.

The song with which the dreamer, earlier in the same dream, realigns his genes — *"I sang to the other inmates of their inner beauty, which realigned my genes and saved me"* — introduces a *genetic eschatology*: the final transformation of the human is not only spiritual but somatic, not only individual but relational (singing to *others* transforms the singer). This is resurrection-physics in embryonic form.

## V. The Defeat of Death: River of Grief as Eschatological Myth

*River of Grief* presents an eschatological *myth* — a narrative that encodes ultimate truth in symbolic form:

> *"Death, the ultimate warlord struck me down in a final battle. According to the Law, Death must pay a blood price or else lose everything to me. Turns out, I'm the Rock of Ages and my bloodguilt price cannot be paid. I, the rock, split in two and, from me, flows a rushing torrent of all the tears of the slain who now belong to me. Death is forever indebted and all death owns, including the souls of the dead, belong to me."*

The eschatological logic is precise. In the ancient world, *blood debt* (bloodguilt) was the ultimate legal obligation: to kill was to incur an irredeemable debt to the slain. If Death kills the one who cannot be owed a blood price — the innocent Rock, the one whose death incurs an infinite obligation — then Death's entire dominion must be surrendered in settlement. This is *Christus Victor* atonement theology (Gustaf Aulén) rendered in mythic terms: Christ's death is not primarily a penal substitution (though that category is present elsewhere) but a *legal trap* that turns Death's victory into its own defeat.

The *rushing torrent of all the tears of the slain* is an image of resurrection as *the vindication of grief*. All who have wept over the dead, all who have died in anguish — their tears are not forgotten but *collected and released* in the final victory. This resonates with Revelation 21:4 (*"He will wipe every tear from their eyes"*) but adds a dimension: the tears are not merely dried but *unleashed as a torrent* — their grief becomes the flood of final justice and new creation.

## VI. The Editors and the End of History: Binary Eschatology

The *Snippets* image of the *Editors* — with their insectoid followers prostrate before a black enthroned figure — presents an alternative eschatological vision, a *counter-eschaton*:

> *"Figures of light, rank after rank, lying prostrate before an enthroned figure of black. Primacy of the Law above all things, perhaps even God."*

This is the *anti-Kingdom*: an end-state of rigid, binary legalism in which even light is subordinated to a dark throne. The ranks of light lying prostrate suggest a failed eschatology — beings of genuine spiritual significance, reduced to submission by a system that has placed Law above Love. This is the eschatological nightmare: not fire and destruction but the cold, clicking efficiency of a universe governed by code without mercy.

The contrast with the *Kingdom* visions of *Dream Prison* and *Herald of the King* is stark: where the Kingdom is characterised by *generosity, awakening, beauty, and the giving of power*, the Editor-realm is characterised by *binary rigidity, submission to the letter, and the erasure of individual complexity*. Eschatologically, the choice before humanity and the spiritual realm is not merely between good and evil but between *life in all its organic complexity* and *death in all its systemic efficiency*.

## VII. Apocalypse as Simulation: The Contingency of the End

The *Apocalypse* dream's final sequence — the dreamer attempting to re-enter the post-apocalyptic simulation only to find *"mod corruption"* — introduces a surprising eschatological note:

> *"Woke up then fell back to sleep. Wanted to re-enter the simulation. Decided to play in London. Load up didn't work, due to mod corruption. Eventually I gave up."*

The *simulation* of the apocalypse is a meta-eschatological comment: the dreamer desires to *experience* the end-times scenario but finds that the simulated version is corrupted. This may encode a genuine theological anxiety: our *models* of the end (our theologies, our eschatological narratives) are themselves susceptible to *mod corruption* — contamination by cultural assumptions, theological distortions, and wishful thinking. The inability to re-enter the simulation is a healthy sign: the end cannot be *played* on one's own terms.

The *EverMind* cosmology extends this: the whole of reality might be a simulation, meaning the *end* of the EverMind would be the *end of everything* for those within it. But the fact that *RealSouls* persist outside the simulation gestures toward a *resurrection theology*: even if the simulated world ends, what is *real* in the persons who inhabit it cannot be simulated away.

## VIII. The Statue of the End Times

In *Prayers of the Saints*, the dreamer observes:

> *"Statue of the End Times, with giant angels and demons fighting it out. A quote along the lines of the 'Enemy Shall Rise'."*

This is eschatological visual theology in concentrated form. The *Enemy Shall Rise* echoes Daniel 8 (the prince who opposes the Prince of Princes), 2 Thessalonians 2 (the man of lawlessness who rises before the Day of the Lord), and Revelation 12–13. The statue format — monumental, public, inscribed — suggests that this eschatological reality is *architecturally permanent*, built into the structure of the city of God as both warning and testimony. The angelic-demonic battle depicted is not yet resolved: the statue shows the conflict *ongoing*, not concluded.

This is honest eschatology: the end is not yet. The Enemy has risen or will rise. The battle continues. The dreamer's role is not to pretend otherwise but to be the *herald* who announces both the conflict's reality and its ultimate resolution in Christ's victory.

## IX. Theological Evolution of Eschatological Thought

**Early phase — Received eschatology**: The dreamer receives standard Christian end-times material (Apocalypse, Forsaken) and adds personal visionary colour, but does not yet substantially revise the inherited framework.

**Middle phase — Corrective prophetic eschatology**: The Chosen Ones dream and Herald of the King explicitly *correct* standard expectation. The Second Coming is different than expected; the messianic category must be pluralised; the Body's awakening is already the beginning of the return.

**Mature phase — Political and cosmic eschatology**: Dream Prison and River of Grief develop the eschatological vision into its political (*Lord Protector*, Kingdom governance) and cosmic (*Death's legal defeat*, the torrent of tears) dimensions. Eschatology is no longer merely about when Christ returns but about *what his return means* for structures of power, the legal status of the dead, and the transformation of creation.

The evolution is from *receiving* end-times imagery to *prophetically revising* it to *participating* in its unfolding.

---

## X. Synthesis

The eschatology of this corpus is characterised by:

- **Proleptic urgency**: the last things are already beginning; the Body's awakening is the first stage of the Return
- **Corporate scope**: the end is not a solitary divine event but the culmination of a collective awakening
- **Political realism**: the Kingdom takes institutional, political, and social form; it does not merely arrive but is *built*
- **Legal precision**: Christ's victory over death is juridically exact — a blood debt that cannot be settled except by total surrender
- **Corrective prophecy**: standard eschatological expectations require revision; the shape of the end is consistently *different than expected*
- **Grief vindicated**: resurrection is not the erasure of suffering but its ultimate validation and release
- **Ongoing conflict**: the end is not yet; the Enemy rises; the battle depicted in the statue is still in progress

This is not armchair eschatology but lived vision — theology received in the night watches and integrated into a life organised around what is coming.
