# Prophecy and Vocation: Calling, Types, and the Prophetic Office

## Overview

The question of personal calling is woven through this entire corpus. The dreamer is not simply a receiver of visions but a figure who is repeatedly *commissioned* — given tasks, assigned roles, sent as herald, appointed as viceroy, rebuked for silence, and corrected when he oversteps. The theology of prophecy and vocation that emerges from these dreams is neither the inflated self-regard of one who imagines himself uniquely chosen nor the false modesty of one who suppresses genuine gifting. It is a theology that holds together *personal particularity* (this specific call, to this specific person, in this specific moment) and *communal generativity* (the call is for others, through others, and ultimately points away from the called one toward Christ).

---

## I. The Logos Before Conversion: Vocation as Discovery, Not Imposition

The beginning of the dreamer's vocational story is told in *Atheist Logos*:

> *"Had a chat about the Logos-Sophia, explained that I believed in the Logos while still an atheist. Trinity of Source, Logos-Sophia, and Presence/Breath. Impersonal principles, not people. Later discovered Jesus as Logos-Sophia through the Gospel of John."*

Vocation here is not an external call descending from outside but an *internal discovery*: the dreamer followed the logic of his own thinking to something structurally equivalent to Christian theology before being told what Christianity says. This is a theology of *prevenient vocation* — God moving in the person's intellectual and spiritual life *before* the person has any religious framework for it. The calling preceded the name; the Logos was present before the Gospel was opened.

This has significant implications for the dreamer's self-understanding as prophet or herald. He is not someone who was given a role from outside and must perform it but someone who was *already living toward something* and then discovered what that something was. His theological articulation is therefore not the performance of a received script but the *naming of his own deepest experience*.

---

## II. The Christ Cycle: Prophecy as Refinement

*Christ Cycle* provides the foundational theology of the prophetic tradition:

> *"Types of Christ and prophets continually born, refining the type, pruning off false Messiahs, false prophets and false prophecies. Truth is distilled by the cycle and the perfect character of God demonstrated. All actions on the crucifixion, even the piercing of Jesus' side, demonstrated to be unintentionally holy, prophetic acts. The facts that this perspective eventually emerge from the prophetic cycle, real or imagined, demonstrated the truth of Christ's life."*

This is a *cumulative* theology of prophecy: revelation is not a single event but a process of *distillation*. Each prophetic generation refines the type, prunes the false, and adds to the accumulating image of what Christ will be. False messiahs and false prophecies are not merely failures; they are *prunings* — each one making clearer what the genuine archetype is not.

The insight about the crucifixion — that *"even the piercing of Jesus' side demonstrated to be unintentionally holy, prophetic acts"* — is theologically striking. The people who pierced Jesus' side were not trying to fulfill prophecy; they were following military procedure. Yet their act unintentionally enacted Zechariah 12:10 (*"they will look on me, the one they have pierced"*) and became the occasion for the water and blood that the church has always understood as symbols of baptism and Eucharist. This is a theology of *unwitting prophecy*: God works through the free actions of agents who do not know they are acting prophetically.

The methodological point — *"the facts that this perspective eventually emerged from the prophetic cycle, real or imagined, demonstrated the truth of Christ's life"* — is an apologetic for the prophetic tradition itself. The mere fact that a coherent and accurate picture of Christ emerged from the fractured, contested, sometimes fraudulent prophetic tradition is itself evidence that something real was being pointed to. Truth is distilled from the process.

---

## III. The Calling Confessed: *Confession of Calling to Dad*

*Confession of Calling to Dad* captures the vocation at its most personal and relational:

> *"Told Dad all about my calling. In return, he told me all about some artwork he'd been doing. He showed me a three-faced painting and half a bust pressed against a mirror. He mentioned Janus."*

The vocational act here is *confession* — telling the calling to another, specifically to the father figure. That the father responds not with disbelief or dismissal but with his own artistic work (*the three-faced painting*) suggests a *mirroring* of vocations: the son's prophetic-spiritual calling and the father's artistic-symbolic work are not as different as they appear. Both are forms of *making meaning visible*, both are oriented toward the threshold (Janus).

The *three-faced painting and half a bust pressed against a mirror* may be the father's unconscious theology: the bust is half-present, half-reflected — the self that is partly in the world and partly in its own reflection. The mirror suggests *self-knowledge* and *double vision*, both of which are prophetic capacities. The father, who in *Jesus Is Lord* is shown to be a *"closet Christian"* consuming enormous amounts of Christian content in secret, may be further along the prophetic-spiritual journey than either he or the dreamer fully realises. The dream suggests that vocation is *communal* — not isolated in the dreamer but distributed across a family system waiting to be integrated.

---

## IV. The Herald: Announcing the Different Return

*Herald of the King* is the most explicit vocational commission in the corpus:

> *"Spreading the good news of the second coming. Different than expected. Children of God are literally the body of Christ and, together, form the Third Temple. The second coming will be preceded by a mass awakening of Christ's body and the Holy Spirit's temple. We will act as one body under Christ, forming the frontline of His kingdom conquest. This means that the first stages of the Second Coming are already underway!"*

The title *Herald* is itself a vocational category: in medieval courts, the herald was the one who announced the King's coming, carried the King's message, and bore the King's authority in that message. The dreamer is specifically called to herald *not* the expected shape of the Second Coming but *its corrected form* — the distributed, corporate awakening that standard eschatological expectation misses.

This is a prophetic vocation with a specifically *corrective* function: not merely announcing what the church already knows but *revising what it thinks it knows*. The classical prophets of Israel had the same function: they did not simply reinforce received tradition but challenged, refined, and sometimes overturned dominant theological expectations in the name of the God those expectations were meant to describe.

The herald's calling is therefore *uncomfortable* — it requires saying *"the standard expectation is wrong"* to people committed to that expectation. The *Chosen Ones* dream shows the cost: even articulating the corrected view requires pushing through what feels like *spiritual choking*.

---

## V. The Chosen Ones and the Mark: Vocation as Plural

*Chosen Ones with the Mark* engages the vocational question most directly in terms of the dreamer's own identity:

> *"I furtively raised my hand... I tried to say 'you have the wrong Mark' but the words caught in my throat... I pushed and bellowed, YOU HAVE THE WRONG MARK... I said some stuff which I can't remember, including something about them interpreting the prophecy wrong and that there are many Chosen Ones with the mark, not just one Chosen Mark."*

The dreamer's furtiveness — the tentative, half-hidden hand-raising — is the appropriate posture of the genuinely called who distrusts the inflation of their own importance. He is not trying to claim the full weight of the Chosen One prophecy; he is trying to *correct* the singularisation of it. The theological move is characteristically humble and structurally correct: *many marked ones*, not *one Mark*. The genuine vocational insight is that the messianic role is *shared* and *distributed*, not monopolised.

The *invisible force* that chokes him when he tries to speak is a consistent feature of prophetic literature: in Jeremiah (*"I do not know how to speak"* — 1:6), in Ezekiel (the scroll-eating and the binding of the tongue — chapters 3–4), in Moses (*"I am slow of speech"* — Exodus 4:10). The prophetic word is resisted, not only by external audiences, but by something internal to the prophetic experience itself. The *bellowing* that breaks through — *YOU HAVE THE WRONG MARK* — is the prophetic word forcing itself past the resistance: the call that cannot ultimately be suppressed.

---

## VI. Divine Plans: Taught by Angels

*Divine Plans* presents the vocational formation at its most mystical:

> *"Reshaping creation according to spoken prompts. Someone was very impressed and referred me to some senior angels. They showed me how they and the Word of God reshapes reality through the spoken and written word. Taught me how to prompt reality. I was amazed but also surprised at how little was prompted, i.e., chaos. Prayer influences the prompts."*

This is the *school of the prophets* in its angelic version. The dreamer is not merely shown what is happening but *taught how to participate* in it. The instruction is specific: the spoken and written word reshapes reality; prayer is the mechanism through which human participation in divine governance becomes operative. This is a *prophetic-intercessory* vocation: the prophet is not merely a mouthpiece but a *co-worker in reality's governance*, whose spoken and written words carry genuine ontological weight.

The humility of the vision — the smallness of the prompting, the vastness of remaining chaos — is a prophetic corrective built into the commission: the dreamer is being taught to participate in divine governance, but he is also being shown how limited that participation is relative to the total scope of reality. Prophetic vocation does not grant omnipotence; it grants *proportionate agency* within a much larger system.

---

## VII. The Viceroy and the Silver Crucifix: The Shape of Prophetic Authority

Two contrasting images define the *form* of the dreamer's vocational authority. In *Dream Prison*:

> *"I reigned as a Vicar in the true sense of the word, like a Viceroy to Christ, restoring his sovereignty while also honouring popular sovereignty."*

And in *Snippets*:

> *"I felt strongly told not to go for the gold of high ecclesiastic office. Around my own neck was a simple silver crucifix."*

The viceroy image is high authority — genuine governing power, direct representation of the King — but exercised in *subordination to the King* and in *service to the people*. The authority is real but its form is *diaconal* (service-oriented) and *transparent* (pointing always to the one it represents, not to itself).

The silver crucifix over the gold is the same theological point rendered as personal symbol: the calling is genuine but its form is *modest*, *direct*, *unadorned*. The gold crucifixes of high office crumble at his touch because they are not his calling — he is not the Archbishop but the herald, not the Lord Protector by ambition but by assignment, not the one sitting on the throne but the one announcing whose throne it is.

---

## VIII. Trumpety Trump: The Danger of the Prophetic Gift

*Trumpety Trump* contains a crucial warning about prophetic vocation:

> *"I was awakening people, rousing their souls to sing, like a conductor. When I did too much, people started chanting 'trumpety trump' and turning on me. I had to calm them and slip away."*

The prophetic calling can *rouse* people to genuine spiritual awakening. But there is a threshold: *too much* rousing, too much activation of the dormant soul, and the energy turns against the rouser. The crowd that was being awakened begins to *chant* — not worship but a derisive, rhythmic chant (*trumpety trump* — mocking the very trumpeting that woke them). The awakener becomes a *target*.

This is the classical prophetic danger: the prophet who is too effective draws not only the love of those he awakens but their *ambivalence and projection*. The crowd wants to make him their leader (*"big, jock types were hunting for me — either to attack me, or worse, make me their leader"*) or to destroy him. The prophetic vocation requires *camouflage* and *proportionality*: not rousing people more than they can bear, not becoming so visible that the mission is derailed by the messenger's prominence.

The conductor image is apposite: a conductor does not play the instruments but *draws the music out*. The moment the conductor becomes more conspicuous than the music, something has gone wrong. Prophetic vocation is fundamentally *theocentric*: it points always to the source, never to itself.

---

## IX. Witness in the City: Post-Encounter Apostolate

After the spiritual combat of *Driving Away Demons*, the dreamer moves through a ruined city:

> *"I told them to remember who is really in charge. God! I regretted not telling them to explicitly call on the name of Jesus."*

The post-combat city is the dreamer's *apostolic field*: having been delivered, he immediately turns to those around him and attempts to share what he knows. The regret — twice noted — about not specifically naming Jesus reflects the prophetic precision that develops through experience: vague theism is insufficient, the Name must be named, the specific good news must be specifically announced.

The *Monkey Junkie* dream extends this: the dreamer shares his *testimony* with a Polish atheist policeman after witnessing a pagan rite. The testimony — his own story of encounter and conversion — is the prophetic tool that replaces argument. He does not debate the policeman into Christianity but *narrates his own experience*, which is the classical prophetic mode: *"I was there; this is what happened; this is what I now know."*

---

## X. Theological Evolution

**Early phase — The call implicit**: The dreamer lives toward something before he can name it. The Logos-Sophia theology, the dream-visions, the sense of appointment — all present before any explicit vocational language.

**Middle phase — The call confessed and tested**: The calling is confessed to the father, tested in spiritual combat, nearly suppressed by the choking force. The prophetic identity is both asserted (YOU HAVE THE WRONG MARK) and refined (many marks, not one).

**Mature phase — The call integrated and exercised**: The dreamer teaches others how to replace counterfeit goods with Spirit-fruit; he aligns to Christ's fractal image and governs as viceroy; he is taught by angels how prayer reshapes reality. The vocational identity is now functionally active, not merely aspirational.

---

## XI. Synthesis

The theology of prophetic vocation in this corpus is characterised by:

- **Prevenience**: the call preceded the conversion; vocation is discovery, not imposition
- **Correctiveness**: the prophetic task includes revising received expectations, not merely reinforcing them
- **Plurality**: the messianic and prophetic roles are distributed, not singular; the called one is always one of many
- **Proportionality**: the conductor must not rouse more than can be borne; the herald must not make himself the message
- **Subordination**: viceroy to Christ, not sovereign; silver crucifix, not gold; the calling always points beyond the caller
- **Apostolic extension**: the calling is never private; after every encounter with the divine, the movement is outward into witness
- **Ordinary means**: testimony, prayer, spoken word, written word — not dramatic miracles but the quiet persistent reshaping of reality through faithful speech

This is a prophetic theology that has learned both to assert and to deflect: to bellow YOU HAVE THE WRONG MARK when the choking force demands silence, and to slip away quietly when the crowd wants to make the herald its idol.

## The Prophetic Poems

The poems give the prophetic vocation its most direct personal articulation — not in the dramatic dream-commissions of *Herald of the King* or *Divine Plans* but in the quieter, more sustained voice of the one who has received the call and is living it out.

*A Name Untold* is the prophetic poem *par excellence* — the narrative of the soul seeking and receiving its vocational identity. The journey through many guises — *"an exile, a knight radiant, a keeper of hearths, / a tender of gardens, a pilgrim of many paths"* — is the prophetic formation in its wandering phase: the Logos-before-conversion period, the years of testing, the inhabiting of multiple identities before the singular one is given. *"What word could compass both light and dark, / earth and sky, and marry contrasts stark? / Chthonic depths and airy heights... / Abstract yet embodied, rooted but upright"* — this is the prophetic question about one's own nature: what name holds the *coniunctio* of everything the wandering has encountered? The question is not rhetorical; the prophet genuinely does not know the answer until it is given.

The arrival of the name — *"The Oaken Pilgrim"* — is the prophetic commission received. Not a dramatic theophany but a *naming*: the oak's rootedness in both earth and sky, its capacity to endure seasons without losing its essential nature, its provision of shelter to others on their own pilgrimages. *"I am given my name"* — passive voice: the name is not achieved or chosen but *received*. This is the prophetic identity in its purest form: not self-designation but vocation accepted from the one who calls. The dreamer who is choked when he tries to speak in *Chosen Ones* and who must bellow through the resistance is, in this poem's quiet ending, finally given the word he needed — not to announce but to *be*.

*Radiant Knight* is the prophetic vocation in its service mode: *"Ready to humbly serve, your radiant knight... Lord of Light, lead me to the weak."* The knight who is radiant is radiant with *borrowed* light — the divine image clarified by alignment to Christ's fractal. The service is specifically directed *to the weak*: this is not the prophet who addresses the powerful from a position of ecclesiastical authority but the herald who finds the forsaken, the dreamer who re-enters the post-apocalyptic sim to rewrite the Bible from memory for the people who think they have been abandoned. *"When under the shadow of death, / I search the darkness for Your light"* — the prophetic vocation leads into darkness, not away from it. The *Trumpety Trump* wisdom is embedded here: the radiant knight serves humbly, seeks the weak, and stays in the shadow until the light is found — never rousing more than can be sustained.

*Pour Out My Soul* is the prophetic act in its most embodied form: *"Pour out my soul for You, / pour out my soul for You / and all people."* The prophetic pouring-out is total self-gift — the soul poured, not merely the voice raised. *"By holy Word I fight / so more may be reborn"* — the prophetic instrument is speech, the *rhema* of God wielded in proclamation. But the speech is preceded by the pouring: only the soul that has been emptied of itself can carry the Word without distortion. *"Upon my head, your mark, / your holy light is mine"* — the prophetic anointing that the *Chosen Ones with the Mark* dream distributes across many is here received personally: the mark on the head, the tinctura of divine light, the vocational seal. The mark does not make the dreamer unique — many carry it — but it makes him *specific*: this particular anointing, for this particular service, to these particular people.

*Since the Beginning* gives the prophetic commission its cosmic scope: *"You were made with a song to sing to me."* The vocation is not an imposition upon a previously purposeless life but the *disclosure* of what the life was always for. The song was always there — in the *Atheist Logos* theology, in the dreams before conversion, in the golden thread that led the exile home — always the soul's song reaching toward its source. The prophetic calling is simply the moment when the song becomes conscious, when the soul discovers that what it has been humming unconsciously is the melody the Logos always intended. *"Join my dance, the song of the harvest, / And New Eden will throng with thanksgiving"* — the prophetic task is ultimately musical: not to argue but to sing, not to compel but to invite others into the dance that is already underway.
