# The Wise Old Man — The Senex and the Source

## Jungian Framework

The Wise Old Man (Senex) is the archetype of meaning, order, and spirit — the inner figure who embodies accumulated wisdom, divine authority, and the capacity to reveal what the ego cannot see from its limited vantage point. He appears when the Hero has exhausted his own resources and requires orientation from a higher source. Jung distinguished between the positive Senex (the guide, mentor, illuminator) and the negative Senex (the tyrant, the ossifier, the authority that demands obedience rather than offering wisdom).

This diary's relationship with the Wise Old Man archetype is unusually rich, partly because the dreamer's own spiritual tradition provides a vivid gallery of such figures — and because the dreams distinguish carefully between those who genuinely guide and those who abuse the authority the archetype confers.

## Jesus as Dual Archetype — Senex and Self

Jesus occupies a unique position in this corpus because he functions simultaneously as the Wise Old Man (guide, protector, source of instruction) and as the Self (the totality toward which individuation moves, as discussed in the Self analysis). The dreams hold both functions without collapsing them.

As Wise Old Man:
- In **Heavenly Ascension**, Jesus carries the dreamer into the heavens, commands the armoured guardians to stand down, and escorts him through the golden citadel — classic mentor functions.
- In **Series Of Doors**, an image of Christ appears at the end of the lost-corridor sequence and the final door opens — the Senex as threshold-opener, providing what the ego's own effort (the *Kyrie Eleison*) alone could not achieve.
- In **Driving Away Demons**, Jesus is invoked by name and the threat is physically repelled — the Wise Old Man as protective power available to the ego through declaration.

As Self (distinct from Senex):
- In **River Of Grief**, the dreamer inhabits a type of Christ — the Rock of Ages — from the inside, experiencing rather than encountering the figure.
- In **Dream Prison**, aligning to Christ's "fractal image" is not a relationship with an external guide but a resonance with a pattern that is both other and interior.

The movement from Senex to Self — from encountering wisdom outside oneself to recognising it within — is the trajectory of individuation. This corpus shows both modes active simultaneously, which suggests a psyche in productive transition.

## God the Father — The Invisible Architect

God the Father appears in a consistent mode: as the originating authority behind the structures the dreamer inhabits, rather than as a figure encountered directly.

In **Supermarket of Sin**, he is "the Founder" — the one who fixes the supply when the manager (the Son) has reformed the staff. He acts at the level of the system, not the individual encounter. He is known by his effects (the atmosphere shifting "from desperate frenzy to bright calm peace") rather than by his face.

In **Divine Plans**, senior angels show the dreamer how "divine plans unfold, reshaping creation through spoken prompts." The dreamer glimpses the mechanics of providence — the Founder's operational logic — and marvels at how little reality is actually prompted, how much remains chaotic. This is the Senex as cosmic architect, whose wisdom is in what he allows as much as what he directs.

In **Future Bugs**, the dreamer speaks to "the God of the Four Skies" — a title that suggests dominion over a multi-dimensional cosmology (the parallel world with humanoid and insectoid species). The Four Skies title places this Senex beyond the single cosmological frame, overarching multiple worlds.

## The Senior Angels — Mediating Wisdom

Between the human dreamer and the divine figures, a consistent tier of senior angelic guides operates as accessible Wise Old Men — beings who have knowledge the dreamer needs and are willing to share it.

**Divine Plans** is the most explicit: senior angels walk the dreamer through the architecture of providence, showing him the relationship between the Word of God, prayer, and the shaping of reality. This is pure Senex function — the experienced guide initiating the younger into understanding.

In **Angels & Demons**, different orders of angels are described with distinct characters: those who delight in the physical world, those who loathe it, holy angels who protect against demonic corruption. The taxonomy itself reflects Senex-level understanding — differentiated, organised, accountable for particular functions.

**Grandad Paddy** shows a figure who is not technically an angel but occupies the same mediating role: the grandfather, still in purgatory, devoting his ongoing existence to intercession for others. He is a Senex in the most human and accessible register — family rather than celestial, flawed rather than perfected, but oriented wholly toward others' good.

## The Negative Senex — Authority Corrupted

The Senex archetype has its shadow form: the elder who does not guide but controls, does not illuminate but demands submission.

The Putin-like dictator in **Museum** is the Negative Senex in its most recognisable political form: a powerful older figure who hunts gifted children to suppress the next generation's potential revolution. He fears what will succeed him. This is the Senex's shadow dynamic — the wisdom-figure calcified into a power-preserver, threatened by the very life he should be nurturing.

The angry older man who assaults the dreamer in **Series Of Doors** — pulled off by an apologetic priest — is a more personal Negative Senex: punitive rather than pedagogical, striking rather than instructing. The priest's apology distinguishes him from the assailant (positive Senex modulating the negative), but the dreamer receives the blow regardless.

The dead church leaders in **Snippets: Going for Silver**, robed in glittering gold whose crucifixes turn black when touched, represent the institutional Senex corrupted: the accrued authority of generations of religious leadership, impressive in appearance, rotten at contact. Their gold is the Negative Senex's characteristic currency — displays of authority that cannot survive genuine examination.

## The Janus Figure — **Confession Of Calling To Dad**

The most intimate Senex-encounter in the corpus involves the dreamer's own father. Dad shows him a three-faced painting and a half-bust pressed against a mirror, mentioning Janus — the two-faced Roman god of thresholds, beginnings, and time. Janus as Senex is a figure who looks both ways simultaneously, who knows what was and what is coming, who stands at the door.

The context is the dreamer telling his father about his spiritual calling — a confession that crosses a family threshold. The fact that the father reaches for the Janus symbol (whether consciously or as a dream-construction) suggests the encounter is genuinely liminal: both men are changing in relation to each other. The father-as-Janus is neither purely positive nor purely negative Senex; he is a threshold-holder, and the dreamer must pass through regardless.

The dreamer's relationship with the Wise Old Man is one of his most developed: he can clearly distinguish the life-giving Senex from its corrupted form, which is itself a mark of individuation — the ability to honour authority without submitting to all who claim it.

## The Wise Old Man in the Poems

Several poems give voice to the Wise Old Man archetype directly — most remarkably, from the inside: the divine Senex himself speaks.

*All of My Creatures* and *Since the Beginning* are both first-person divine addresses — the Wise Old Man's own voice, offering what the Senex offers: orientation, perspective, the long view. "I waited since the beginning for you, / From speaking light into being, / I counted down every second until / I could tell you how loved you are!" (*Since the Beginning*) — this is the Senex at his most purely positive: not demanding submission but offering intimacy. "I am Alpha and Omega, beginning and end. / Morning Star and the dark shade of evening / My being is the being in all things" — the Senex as cosmic ground, the ordering principle that encompasses rather than excludes.

*All of My Creatures* extends this into invitation: "Join the heavenly family, / love forever, undyingly." The divine Senex here does not rule from a distance but gathers — the Founder of *Supermarket of Sin* is not remote but intimately known in every creature, every moment. "I created the stars and the Sun, / so you'd find me in every one" echoes the *Future Bugs* dream's "God of the Four Skies" — a Senex whose sovereignty extends across multiple registers of reality.

*For Love, In Love, He Made You* presents the Senex as the origin of the dreamer's particular soul: "Since before the beginning of time, / He's had you in His holy mind. / The Word plotted out your soul's climb, / from beginning to end, Him to find." This is the Senex as the architect of personal destiny — not merely cosmic order but intimate foreknowledge. "The Word plotted out your soul's climb" places the Senex in exact alignment with *Divine Plans*' senior angels showing the dreamer how spoken prompts reshape creation: in both cases, the Logos is the operative principle.

*My Peace I Give to You* gives the Senex-Christ in his most accessible register: "My peace I give to you, peace be with you! / Deepest Shalom, beyond earthly knowing." The Shalom offered is not merely comfort but ontological reorientation — the peace that "passes understanding" is the Senex's gift of a perspective the ego cannot generate from its own resources. "Come to My Father boldly, the curtain is torn" — the Senex's ultimate threshold-dissolution, the boundary between human and divine permanently opened by an act the ego did not and could not perform.
