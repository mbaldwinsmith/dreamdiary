# The Trickster — The Boundary-Crosser Who Reveals by Subverting

## Jungian Framework

The Trickster is the archetype of paradox, disruption, and transgression. He is simultaneously fool and sage, subversive and creative, destructive and catalytic. Jung associated the Trickster with the shadow side of the Senex (the wise authority figure gone anarchic) and with the figure who crosses all boundaries — between worlds, categories, and roles — precisely because those boundaries need to be crossed for transformation to occur.

What distinguishes the Trickster from the simple villain is that his disruptions, however uncomfortable, tend to expose what needs exposing. He does not create the problem; he reveals the problem that was already there. After a Trickster encounter, the dreamer is wiser — usually despite himself.

## The Bible Scholar — *Driving Away Demons*

The clearest Trickster in the corpus is the Bible scholar the dreamer meets on the woodland path in **Driving Away Demons**. He has the markers of Senex authority — scholarly knowledge, apocryphal expertise, a confident demeanour — but when the hulking Shadow-figure approaches, he reveals himself:

> *"The Bible scholar discusses apocrypha with the dreamer confidently, but when the hulking figure approaches, panics, sweats with fear he denies, and ultimately abandons the dreamer to run into the dark forest."*

The Trickster's signature move: present as wise, authoritative, equipped — then dissolve at the moment of real demand. The scholar is not a liar exactly; he genuinely knows the apocrypha. But his knowledge is Persona-deep, not Self-deep. It cannot withstand the approach of the unconscious (the hulking figure).

His flight into the dark forest is richly symbolic: he disappears into the very territory from which the threat emerged. The Trickster, ultimately, belongs to the shadow-world he claims to navigate from the outside.

What the scholar reveals by failing: the dreamer cannot rely on learned authority when facing the deep unconscious. He must call on something more immediate and personal — which is exactly what he does, invoking Jesus directly.

## The Atheist Catholic — *Monkey Junkie*

The Polish policeman in **Monkey Junkie** is a gentler but equally pointed Trickster. He is explicitly described as an "atheist Catholic" — a walking self-contradiction, a man whose institutional identity (Catholic) is disowned by his own professed belief (atheist). He has a "long conversation" with the dreamer who shares testimony.

The Trickster quality here is in the category-violation: atheist Catholics should not exist, and yet they do, and the policeman is completely sincere in both positions. He is professionally charged with keeping order (among Norse monkey-vikings, no less — a supremely absurd Trickster setting) while being internally incoherent about the deepest questions of meaning.

His function in the dream: to create the space for the dreamer's testimony. The Trickster, by embodying contradiction, draws out the dreamer's clarity. You cannot explain your faith casually to someone who is simultaneously a believer and a non-believer — you have to think about what you actually mean.

## The Hidden Performers — *Jesus Is Lord*

**Jesus Is Lord** opens with "hidden performers gradually revealing themselves" in a megachurch auditorium. This is Trickster energy in a collective form: the audience believes it is watching one kind of event and discovers it is watching another. The performers were always there; the revelation is a staged unmasking.

The Trickster's gift here is disruption of the expected: what looked like a passive assembly turns out to be an active performance, which then bleeds into the dreamer's experience of his own family (who also turn out to be performing — the dad's "closet Christian" habits, the family's aggression when "Jesus is Lord" is spoken, the needle-mouthed woman offering a depression injection).

The Trickster in this dream does not distinguish cleanly between sacred and sinister — the hidden performers could be angels or provocateurs. That ambiguity is characteristic: the Trickster operates in the space where categories break down.

## The Needle-Mouthed Woman — *Jesus Is Lord*

The woman with a needle for a mouth who lunges at the dreamer in **Jesus Is Lord** is a Trickster-Shadow hybrid. She operates through the promise of help (an injection implies medicine) that turns out to be a threat. The concealed weapon inside the offer of assistance is a classic Trickster inversion. The dreamer pushes a man in her path and runs — he outmanoeuvres her, which is also characteristic: Trickster encounters are resolved through wit and agility, not force.

## The Planted Audience — *Jesus Is Lord*

The "menacing figures surrounding the dreamer and offering a 'depression injection'" constitute a collective Trickster-Shadow: they present as helpers (the injection, the crowd around a person in distress) but intend harm. Their menace is in the mimicry of care — the Trickster's deepest move, which is to put on the face of what you need most in order to neutralise your defences.

## The Jester — *Child Shadow Integration: Storming the Castle*

The Jester of **Storming the Castle** is the most purely archetypal Trickster in the entire corpus, and his method is more sophisticated than any prior figure.

The setup is an apparently straightforward Hero-sequence: forcefield, crumbling castle, challenging knight, sword duel. The dreamer wins. Then:

> *"Knight says while we were fighting the jester was castrating me."*

The Jester never appears in direct conflict. He is not on the battlefield. He operates in the social register — spreading rumours, eroding the dreamer's masculine credibility and authority — while the dreamer's attention is entirely consumed by the combat the Trickster arranged for exactly that purpose. The knight was a distraction. The Jester is the real adversary, and he wins by never being seen.

This is the Trickster at his most architecturally sophisticated: he does not confront the Hero, he *stages* a confrontation the Hero will readily engage with, then works the blind spot that engagement creates. The hero's strength — willingness to fight — is weaponised against him. The dreamer tries to will the subsequent images of humiliation away through semi-lucid intent. He cannot. The Trickster's work is already done; it cannot be unwilled.

The castration imagery is precise. In dream symbolism, castration is not primarily sexual — it is the severing of generative authority, the removal of the power to create, to act, to be taken seriously. The Jester attacks not the dreamer's body but his standing in the communal field: his reputation, his credibility, his masculine dignity as recognised by others. This is the Trickster's preferred target. He rarely destroys directly; he undermines the conditions that make power possible.

What the Jester reveals: the Hero cannot win by fighting what the Trickster presents. The combat is always, in part, misdirection.

## The Squid Game Setting — *Squid Game*

**Squid Game** presents perhaps the most philosophically explicit Trickster dream in the corpus:

> *"Actually totally random but people imagined patterns and set up elaborate games."*

The Trickster here is not a person but reality itself: events are random, but the human need for pattern (for a Senex who knows what he is doing, for a rules-based cosmos) projects elaborate structure onto pure chaos. The game show / escape room framing is entirely constructed by the participants. The Trickster in this reading is the human pattern-making impulse itself — the thing that convinces us there are rules when there may be none.

This is the most philosophically troubling Trickster moment in the diary, because it momentarily brackets the dreamer's entire theological framework (divine providence, a cosmos shaped by spoken prompts) and asks: what if it's all a squid game?

The fact that the dreamer ends up in a church with his team suggests the dream doesn't fully surrender to that nihilism — but the question is genuinely posed.

## Summary

The Trickster in this corpus:

| Figure | Mechanism | Dream | Revelation |
|--------|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Bible scholar | Presents as learned, flees at real threat | *Driving Away Demons* | Learned authority fails; direct invocation remains |
| Atheist Catholic policeman | Self-contradicting believer/non-believer | *Monkey Junkie* | Contradiction creates space for genuine testimony |
| Hidden performers | Staged unmasking of the expected | *Jesus Is Lord* | Nothing in the social field is as it appears |
| Needle-mouthed woman | Help that is a weapon | *Jesus Is Lord* | Care can be weaponised; wit is the correct counter |
| The Jester | Stages combat as distraction; operates in blind spot | *Child Shadow Integration* | The Hero's strength is the Trickster's best weapon against him |
| Reality itself | Randomness projected as pattern | *Squid Game* | The most unsettling Trickster needs no face |

Across these appearances the Trickster performs his essential function: he dismantles false certainty. The bible scholar dismantles intellectual authority; the policeman dismantles ideological coherence; the performers dismantle social legibility; the Jester dismantles heroic efficacy by revealing that the battlefield the Hero won was not the real battlefield; the Squid Game dismantles cosmic order. Each dismantling, uncomfortable as it is, leaves the dreamer with more reliable footing than the assumption it removed.
