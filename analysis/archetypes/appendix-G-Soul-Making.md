# Appendix G — Soul-Making: James Hillman's Archetypal Psychology

## Theoretical Background

James Hillman broke most sharply from Jung's mainstream of any major post-Jungian theorist. Where Jung's model is fundamentally developmental — individuation as a journey toward the Self, the ego learning to relate rightly to the unconscious, the goal being wholeness — Hillman proposed what he called **Archetypal Psychology**: a polytheistic, image-centred approach in which the soul is not journeying anywhere, but *deepening*.

Hillman's key contributions, developed across *Re-Visioning Psychology* (1975), *The Dream and the Underworld* (1979), and *A Blue Fire* (collected essays), include:

**Soul-making** (*poiesis*): the soul's primary activity is not progress toward integration but the endless making of images — the creative, imaginal process by which experience is deepened rather than resolved. A dream is not a problem to be decoded and resolved; it is an image to be lived with, explored, and allowed to deepen.

**The polytheistic psyche**: rather than a single Self toward which all energy moves, Hillman proposed a multiplicity of autonomous inner figures — gods, not a God. Each archetype (Ares, Aphrodite, Hermes, Hades) represents a genuine mode of being, equally valid, none reducible to the others or to a single overarching principle.

**Pathologising as soul-work**: Hillman argued against the therapeutic impulse to heal symptoms. The pathological images (the monsters, the grotesque, the darkness) are not problems to be resolved but are themselves the soul's most active site of work. To cure the symptom prematurely is to interrupt the soul-making process.

**The Underworld imagination**: Hades/the Underworld as the realm of depth, image, and the dead — not a place to escape but a perspective to cultivate. The dreamer who goes down (into darkness, failure, the uncanny) is doing soul-work that the upward spiritual journey (toward light, integration, the Self) cannot do.

**The autonomous image**: dream figures are not symbols for something else; they are autonomous presences with their own intentions, not messengers from the dreamer's unconscious delivering content for the ego to decode.

---

## Where Hillman Challenges This Corpus

Hillman's framework is in genuine tension with the dreamer's theological commitments, and naming that tension is more useful than pretending it doesn't exist.

The dreamer's response to his dreams is consistently *interpretive and integrative*: demons are externalised Shadow-material to be driven back and integrated; the Anima is moving through developmental stages toward Sophia; the individuation arc is progressing. This is recognisably Jungian and deeply influenced by the tradition's therapeutic, developmental logic.

Hillman would push back: *are the demons only Shadow-material?* What if they are autonomous presences with their own logic that the "drive them back and integrate" framework forecloses? What if the red-haired woman of *Morning Dew* is not "the Anima at the Eve stage" but an irreducible figure in her own right — not a stage to be passed through but a mode of being to be inhabited? What if the boar-person of *Breaking Out* is not the Shadow erupting through the body but a genuine animal-being making its own claim?

These are not rhetorical questions. They open the corpus to a different kind of attention.

## Soul-Making in Practice — What the Corpus Already Does

Despite the theological tension, several features of this diary embody Hillman's soul-making sensibility whether or not the dreamer intends them that way.

### Staying with the Image

The lucid dream practice — rubbing surfaces, touching walls, saying the Jesus Prayer to *maintain presence in the dream* — is functionally identical to Hillman's injunction to stay with the image rather than waking from it (literally or metaphorically). The dreamer who anchors himself in the dreamscape rather than pulling back into waking consciousness is practicing the soul-making discipline: refusing premature resolution, staying with the uncanny, keeping company with the image until it has delivered more than its first impression.

**The Restaurant** and **Lucid City and Beach** demonstrate this most clearly: the dreamer moves through procedural dream-cities, touching surfaces, watching words change when approached too closely (a characteristically Hillman-resonant image — language that is only stable at a distance, that dissolves under the Magician's close inspection), and staying curious rather than purposive. These dreams are not going anywhere; they are deepening.

### Pathologising as Soul-Work

**Breaking Out**'s sequence is the corpus's richest Hillman-text. The dreamer becomes a boar-person with tusks and a barbed penis; metal shards appear in his mouth; lab-coat figures attack. This is pathologising at its most visceral — the body transformed into something monstrous, unpleasant, transgressive. Hillman would resist the impulse to "decode" this as Shadow eruption requiring integration. He would ask instead: what is the boar's perspective? What does it mean to *be* tusked, barbed, mouthy with metal? What is the soul-work being done by this specific grotesque, and what is lost if we resolve it too quickly into a lesson about the animal nature requiring containment?

The sequence in **Dream Prison** where the dreamer emerges covered in mouths is similarly Hillmanesque. The image is not merely disturbing — it is *excessive*, surrealist, resistant to clean symbolic reduction. Covered in mouths: each mouth speaks, eats, forms words, breathes. The multiplicity of mouths is not explicable as "the repressed finally speaking" without remainder. There is something in the image beyond its decoding. Hillman would say: live with the mouths. Let them speak without immediately deciding what they mean.

### The Underworld Imagination

**Purgatory** and **Grandad Paddy** are the corpus's most sustained engagements with what Hillman would call the Underworld imagination — the perspective of the dead, the realm of depth and slow time. Hillman argued that the modern psyche has largely lost contact with this perspective: we treat the dead as simply gone, and we treat dreams of the dead as symbolic material rather than as genuine encounters in a different mode of being.

The dreamer's encounters with Nan and Grandad Paddy are treated with extraordinary literal seriousness: Nan says she is in purgatory for six years, complains about specific social wounds, shows her broken rosary. Paddy intercedes ceaselessly for others. These are not "symbols" in any reductive sense — they are presences encountered in a depth-register that the dreamer takes at face value. This is the Underworld imagination functioning: the living dreamer travelling to the realm of the dead and bringing back intelligence that matters.

Hillman would also note the theological colour of these encounters. *Purgatory* is not a Jungian category; it is a Catholic one. But Hillman's archetypal psychology deliberately drew on pre-Christian mythology (Hades, Persephone, Psyche) to recover the Underworld imagination that Christianity had tended to collapse into heaven-and-hell binary. The purgatorial grey zone — neither paradise nor damnation, not resolved — is precisely the Underworld imagination the dreamer's cultural tradition makes available to him.

### The Shadow That Speaks — *Child Shadow Integration*

**Child Shadow Integration: Confronting the Twisting Shadow** is the corpus's richest site of tension between the Jungian developmental reading and Hillman's soul-making sensibility.

The Jungian reading is compelling and internally complete: the dreamer becomes lucid, moves toward the Shadow with compassion rather than combat, sits with it, says "I love you," and the Shadow dissolves. Integration achieved. The mechanism is named, the resolution is felt, the dreamer wakes transformed — physically holding himself, continuing the work in waking life through spontaneous apology and stinging tears. This is individuation functioning as the model predicts.

Hillman would not dispute any of that. He would ask a different question: *what about the shadow's cry itself?*

> *"Nobody likes me."*

The Jungian reading hears this as the wounded inner child's core belief — depression's voice, the thing the ego left behind in adolescence. This is certainly true. But Hillman would resist the move that happens immediately afterward: the shadow "turns into a watery blob and slowly dissipates." The wound speaks, is met with love, and dissolves. It is resolved.

Hillman's pathologising principle would slow down here and ask: *what is the soul-work that was being done by this specific complaint, in this specific voice, before it was dissolved?* "Nobody likes me" is not merely a symptom to be cured. It is a perspective — the perspective of the rejected, the unloved, the one who has learned from long experience in the dark that connection does not reach it. What does it know that the integrated ego will now not know? What is lost when the shadow that wept is healed?

This is not an argument against the integration. It is an argument for staying, briefly, with what the shadow was — not just with what the shadow needed. The dragon in *Monster Baby* had nowhere to roost. Now it has been given a home. But the dragon also knew something about homelessness that the settled ego will not. The soul-making question is whether that knowledge survives the integration, or is dissolved with the watery blob.

The dreamer's waking tears — "stinging," piercing, defensive — suggest something of that knowledge *does* survive: the ache of recognising how long that part was left alone is itself a form of ongoing acquaintance with the shadow's experience, even after the shadow has dissolved. The grief is the shadow's posthumous teaching.

## The Polytheistic Psyche and This Corpus

The tension between Hillman's polytheism and the dreamer's monotheism is real but not absolute. The dreamer's Christ-centred theology insists on a single ultimate source (the Founder, God the Father, the Self as Christ-symbol); Hillman's archetypal psychology insists on irreducible plurality (many gods, no one archetype subordinate to another).

What the corpus actually does, however, is more complex than strict monotheism. The dreamer occupies *multiple* archetypal positions across different dreams: King-Elder, Warrior-knight, Magician-herald, Lover-swimmer, Puer-visionary. These are not roles the ego adopts in sequence; they co-exist as simultaneous modes of being, each emerging in the appropriate context. This is functionally polytheistic even if theologically monotheist — the psyche is plurally inhabited while the theology insists on a single ground.

Hillman would say: exactly. The gods are not in competition with God; they are the *modes of being* through which the one is experienced by the many. The dreamer moves through Ares-energy (Warrior), Hermes-energy (Magician-herald), Eros-energy (Lover), Zeus-energy (King), Apollo-energy (the prophetic dreamer). None of these is reducible to "the Self" without losing something essential about what each is.

## Where Soul-Making Adds to the Analysis

Hillman's framework contributes several things to this corpus that the standard Jungian model does not easily provide:

**Permission to not resolve**: The dreams that resist clean interpretation — the boar-person, the mouths, the editors with black crackling light, the insectoid beings — may be most honoured by sitting with their strangeness rather than decoding it into therapeutic content. The analysis files in this collection have largely tried to decode; this appendix offers the alternative.

**Attention to the image's own logic**: Rather than asking "what does the cobra mean?" the soul-making question is "what does the cobra do?" How does it behave, what is its relationship to the vault it guards, what kind of encounter does it make possible? The cobra that is "not particularly scary" is doing something specific — it is calm, it acknowledges the dreamer, it does not charge. That behaviour is the image's own contribution, not reducible to what it symbolises.

**The value of descent**: The corpus's most uncomfortable dreams — *Breaking Out*, *Dream Prison*, *Purgatory*, *Cycles Of Dream Prisons* — are not failed spiritual experiences that should have been more luminous. They are Underworld work: the soul's necessary descents into the dark, the grotesque, the stuck. Hillman would insist these are as valuable as the heavenly ascents — perhaps more so, because they are the places where spiritual inflation cannot survive.

## Summary

Hillman's Archetypal Psychology is in genuine tension with the dreamer's theological frame — but a productive tension:

| Jungian/developmental reading | Hillmanesque/soul-making reading |
|-------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| Demons = externalised Shadow to be driven back | Demons = autonomous presences with their own logic |
| Red-haired woman = Anima at Eve stage | Red-haired woman = irreducible figure in her own right |
| Boar-person = Shadow eruption requiring integration | Boar-person = animal-being making its own claim |
| Mouths = repressed speaking | Mouths = surrealist image to be inhabited, not decoded |
| Lucid practice = ego maintaining awareness | Lucid practice = soul-making, staying with the image |
| Purgatory = liminal archetype | Purgatory = Underworld imagination, the dead encountered literally |

This analysis does not argue that Hillman's reading supersedes the Jungian one. It argues that both are available, and that a corpus as rich as this one benefits from the friction between them. The developmental model asks: *where is this going?* The soul-making model asks: *what is this, right now, in its full strangeness?* The most honest answer to both questions produces the deepest reading of the diary.
