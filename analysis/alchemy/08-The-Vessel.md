# The Vessel: The Hermetic Container

## Definition

The *vas hermeticum* — the hermetic vessel — is one of alchemy's most important concepts. The Work cannot be performed in the open air; it requires a sealed container in which the volatile substances are held under pressure, unable to escape, forced to interact until transformation occurs. The vessel must be strong enough to contain the most extreme conditions (fire, pressure, volatility) without shattering; it must be transparent enough for the alchemist to observe the Work; and it must be hermetically sealed — hence *hermetic*, from Hermes, who was said to have discovered the secret of sealing vessels with wax.

The alchemical vessel is simultaneously *practical* (the actual glass or ceramic container) and *symbolic* (the psyche, the body, the relationship, the sacred space in which transformation occurs). Jung wrote that *the vas hermeticum is the uterus and the grave in one* — the space of gestation and the space of dissolution, where the new life emerges from the death of the old.

A crucial feature of the vessel: it cannot be too large. The Work requires *concentration*. A vessel too broad disperses the volatile elements and nothing transforms. The alchemical discipline of *containment* — holding the tension without release, staying in the vessel even when the pressure becomes extreme — is among the hardest of all the disciplines.

This dream corpus is extraordinarily rich in vessel imagery. The dreamer returns again and again to the question of what constitutes an adequate container for the Work — and what happens when the vessel breaks, leaks, or proves too small.

## The Simulation as Vessel

### *Dream Prison* and *EverMind*

The most architecturally developed vessel in the corpus is the simulation — the enclosed reality-system in which souls are contained, tested, and potentially transformed. In *Dream Prison*, the *"shoddy sim of reality"* run by a corporation is a failed vessel: it is built for imprisonment and exploitation rather than transformation. The dreamer's years of *"stretching the bounds of the sim"* are the alchemist pressing against the walls of an inadequate vessel — until it cracks and the transformation can begin.

The *EverMind* is a more sophisticated vessel: the ultimate simulation cluster containing billions of souls in their *SimWorlds*. Each *SimWorld* is a nested vessel within the larger vessel. The Sourceress's *BabyLondon* — a world built inside the world — is a vessel within a vessel within a vessel: the alchemical concept of *nested containment*, the *mise en abyme* of the Work. The tragedy of BabyLondon is precisely a vessel problem: it was built as a tribute to celebrity culture (a false gold) and could not transform its inhabitants until the *CodeSong* crack appeared and real beauty flooded in.

### *Apocalypse*

The *"simulation"* the dreamer wishes to re-enter — specifically *loading up London as a game* — presents the dream itself as an explicitly chosen vessel. The dreamer recognises the simulation as *optional* and *mutable*: he can choose the parameters, the setting, the companions. The mod corruption that prevents re-entry is the vessel-integrity problem: a contaminated vessel cannot perform the Work reliably.

The Forsaken world of the far future is, in contrast, an involuntary vessel: the dreamer is *sucked in* through the vortex, carried into a context he did not choose. This is the vessel imposing itself on the alchemist — the psyche's sovereign claim on the Work, regardless of the ego's preferences.

## The Dream Itself as Vessel: Lucid Container

A recurring feature of the corpus is the dreamer's conscious engagement with the *dream as vessel* — the practice of maintaining the container through deliberate attention.

### *Breaking Out*, *The Restaurant*, *Lucid City and Beach*

*"I kept rubbing my hands together or on surfaces to keep my senses immersed in the dream."*
*"I touched surfaces to keep myself in the dream."*
*"I sang and moved my body to keep myself immersed in the dream world."*

These are all vessel-maintenance practices. The lucid dreamer faces the constant risk of *waking* — the vessel fracturing and the volatile substance dissipating. The techniques of surface-touching, hand-rubbing, singing, and body-movement are the alchemical equivalent of *banking the fire*: keeping the temperature within the vessel at the right level to sustain the Work without allowing the contents to boil off into waking.

The liminal state recorded in *Breaking Out* — *"In between dreams, I drift, holding on to the dream state. I quietly demand an apology and/or explanation. I gradually become more awake but I continuously rehearse the dream details, so I wouldn't forget them."* — is the conscious preservation of the vessel's contents after the container has dissolved. The dreamer uses memory as a secondary vessel to hold the Work's findings until they can be *coagulated* in the notebook.

### *Cycles of Dream Prisons*

*"Continual rising and falling from lucidity, having to decipher codes and reminders for Waking Up again and again."*

The cycling in and out of the vessel is the alchemical risk of *imperfect sealing*. The phallus as a *recurring reminder* is the body's way of maintaining vessel integrity when the mind would drift: the *sal* principle keeping the dreamer moored to the physical container.

*"Too much lucidity, and others in the dreamscape would turn on me."* — This is the vessel's pressure dynamic: too much awareness of the vessel's constructed nature and the collective within it becomes hostile. The vessel requires both the dreamer's *full engagement* (staying immersed) and *appropriate restraint* (not exposing the construction too aggressively to the other contents).

## Sacred Architecture as Vessel

### *Renewal Dream* — The Church with the Swimming Pool

*"The Church generated its own energy, had a lively social presence, and even had its own swimming pool."*

The Church as vessel is a recurring image in the corpus. Here it generates its own energy — the *athanor*, the alchemical furnace that maintains its own heat — and contains the *solutio* (the swimming pool) within itself. The dreamer's failure to remove his shoes before entering the pool — *"A Knight muddied the floor without washing his feet"* — is a vessel-violation: the introduction of external contamination into the purifying space. His rationalisation (*"my feet are much cleaner than many peoples'"*) rather than confession is the ego's characteristic resistance to vessel discipline: the claim that the container's requirements don't apply to oneself.

### *Prayers of the Saints* — The Flying Buttresses

*"A vivid image of a gothic church, supported by two flying buttresses, representing both of our prayers edifying and supporting the body of Christ."*

The gothic church as vessel is one of the most theologically precise images in the corpus. Flying buttresses — those external arching supports that allow a gothic cathedral to reach unprecedented height without collapsing under its own weight — are external to the vessel but essential to its structural integrity. The interpretation that these buttresses represent *mutual prayer* is exact: the vessel of the Church is maintained not by its own internal strength alone but by the supporting prayers of those outside it. The vessel is held up by relationship.

### *Supermarket of Sin* — "The Deli Counter and the Fruit Aisle"

The supermarket is a degraded vessel: built to contain nourishment but co-opted to distribute poison. The new manager's revelation — *"there's already a deli counter and a fruit aisle, but almost no one goes there"* — is the vessel's redeeming insight: even in the corrupted container, the genuine goods have been present all along. The Work does not always require a new vessel — sometimes it requires the *rediscovery* of what the existing vessel was originally built to hold.

## The Body as Vessel

### *Series of Doors* — "Your Weight Will Kill You"

The warning handed to the dreamer on a slip of paper is a vessel-integrity warning: *the body as vessel is under strain*. The alchemical Work cannot proceed if the physical container is compromised. Weight here carries both literal and symbolic meaning: the *gravitational burden* of unresolved material pressing down on the physical form.

### *Dream Prison* — The Body of Mouths

The body covered in mouths — *"mouths including my eyes becoming a pair of mouths and one huge mouth covering the front of my head"* — is the vessel at maximum transformation: the container itself has become *entirely permeable*, every surface a threshold, every organ a speech organ. This is the vessel at the point of rupture preceding rubedo: not shattering but *flowering*, opening in every direction to allow the transformed substance to flow out and in simultaneously.

### *Temptation in the Garden*

The garden — one of alchemy's most ancient vessel symbols, the *hortus conclusus* (enclosed garden) of the Song of Songs — is under siege. The paralysed dreamer repeating the Jesus Prayer while demonic figures circle him but cannot enter is the alchemical sealing of the vessel at its most tested: the contents are under maximum pressure from the outside, but the *hermetic seal* — the Name — holds.

## The Purgatorial Vessel: *Between Worlds*

### *Purgatory*

The liminal space of Purgatory — resembling the grandparents' house yet clearly *other* — is the vessel between vessels: the containing space between the nigredo of earthly life and the rubedo of Paradise. The grandmother's broken rosary — *"possibly indicating that souls in purgatory can't pray for themselves"* — is the vessel's fundamental constraint: in purgatory, the container does not generate its own fire. Heat must be supplied from outside, through the prayers of the living. The dreamer's role as intercessor is the alchemist supplying heat to a vessel that cannot sustain its own temperature.

### *Grandad Paddy*

*"Attended Holy Mass in heaven with Pope Benedict and the saints... Grandad Paddy was stood at her right shoulder constantly praying for her."*

The heavenly liturgy is the ultimate vessel: the eternal container in which the Work of the whole creation is continuously sustained. Grandad Paddy's ceaseless intercession, even from his own purgatorial position, is the vessel serving as *conduit*: a soul in one vessel praying for a soul in another, maintaining the flow of grace across the threshold.

## Summary: The Properties of the Vessel in This Corpus

The ideal vessel, as it emerges from these dreams, has the following properties:

| Property | Requirement | Dream Instance |
|---|---|---|
| **Strength** | Withstand pressure without shattering | Dream Prison (surviving years of stretching) |
| **Transparency** | Allow the Work to be observed | Lucid dreaming practices |
| **Sealed integrity** | Prevent contamination and leakage | Temptation in the Garden; Renewal Dream (feet) |
| **Self-sustaining heat** | Generate or maintain transformative energy | Church with swimming pool; Living Mass |
| **External support** | Structural support from outside | Flying buttresses as mutual prayer |
| **Proper scale** | Neither too large (dispersive) nor too small (explosive) | Trumpety Trump (too much at once) |

The vessel is not passive. It *participates* in the Work — it is not merely the container but a co-agent of transformation. The dreamer's repeated return to questions of containment, sealing, integrity, and permeability suggests that vessel-consciousness is one of the deepest preoccupations of this alchemical life.
